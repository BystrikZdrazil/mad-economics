# Key challenges and opportunities
- Traditionally there is lean production, in more recent times combined with agile and green philosophy
- There are more and more technologies introduced
	- This means rising complexity, volatility and speed
- Having large inventories can sometimes be a bad idea, as the more inventory a company has, the less flexible they are

- Designing and optimising networks; understanding network characteristics, behaviours and complexities
- Pursuit of strategies based around lean and agile principles and varying combinations of both
- Mass customisation/postponement/delayed configuration/additive manufacturing
- Time compression – faster order-to-delivery cycles and elimination of non-value-adding time
- Developing value-adding logistics activities
- Managing reverse logistics flows
- Moving from linear to circular economy business models
- Coordinating and managing transport flows and directional imbalances; selecting modes and routes

# Non-financial reporting directive
The EU has adopted several directives and regulations on climate accounting and reporting of greenhouse gas emissions.

One of the most important instruments in this area is the EU’s Directive on the disclosure on the non-financial information (*2014/95/EU*), also known as the Non-Financial Reporting (*NFR*) Directive.

This directive requires certain large companies to disclose information about their policies, practices, and results regarding environmental matters, social conditions, human rights, and the fight against corruption and bribery.

## From NFRD to CSRD
Like the NFRD, the newest addition is a directive called the Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive, which also focuses on reporting non-financial elements. However, this directive is aimed at even more companies (*Eventually also SMEs*)

### Start of reporting
- 2027 - Large EU companies
- 2028 - Non-EU ultimate parent undertaking
	- Companies generating more than 450€ millions in revenue and has an EU branch with 200€ million revenue
- 2027 (*earliest*) - Wave-one companies
	- All other publicly listed larger companies with more than 500 employees

# CSRD
When reporting under the CSRD framework, companies must use the GHG protocol where three scores have been defined.
- **Scope 1** - Direct emissions from company-owned or controlled resources
- **Scope 2** - All greenhouse gas emissions that arises from production of energy purchased from another party
	- Electricity, heat, cooling
- **Scope 3** - All indirect emissions that occur as a result of the company’s operations
	- Emissions caused by the supply chain, the use of the products by end consumers, waste treatment

# Double materiality
The new sustainability regulation, CSRD, defines a detailed method for assessing which information must be included in the sustainability reporting of the largest companies. This method is called a double materiality assessment.

Even though the Omnibus has excluded a large part of companies that are required to implement CSRD, it is still a requirement that often works as a snowball effect.

When bigger companies are required to document scope 3, it will affect all the partners in the supply chian. So even if there isnt  a EU directive stating the need for non-financial reporting, many smaller companies might be affected by it via supply chain partners.

Companies can use the principles from a double materiality assessment to identify and gain an overview of which sustainability areas may be material for your company and for the surrounding society.

> [!example]
> A company has conducted an employee satisfaction survey that shows the employees are unsatisfied and demotivated
> 
> - **The traditional  materiality perspective**
> 	- Unproductive employees affect the company’s revenue (*outside-in perspective*)
> - **Double materiality perspective**
> 	- Unproductive employees affect the company’s revenue (*outside in perspective*)
> 	- The company’s management affects the company’s satisfaction (*inside-out perspective*)

# Circular economy and reverse logistics
The idea of a **circular economy** has been gaining traction in recent years, alongside the growing focus on sustainability.

In **Supply Chain Management** (*SCM*), however, the term “**reverse logistics**” has existed for many years and refers to the systems and processes involved when, for example, customers return products.

A circular economy goes a step further, as it also places strong emphasis on **waste management**, **reuse of materials**, and **repairs**.

## Circular economy
A **circular economy** can therefore be seen as an additional link in the supply chain that companies increasingly need to incorporate.
- How do we physically transport the products or parts that need to be returned through the supply chain?
- How do we coordinate this across multiple parties in a supply chain?
- Can we expect that circular processes may need to take place in more places than “just” from the end user, perhaps also through suppliers or sub-suppliers?

Overall, the need for **innovation and new ways of thinking** within SCM is increasing.

# The future
Developments in the world (and in SCM in general) have created a growing need for leaders and employees who have a broad understanding and knowledge across several different areas.

Traditionally, there has been a strong demand for specialists, but the focus is now increasingly on generalists.

According to Christopher, however, the two should be combined in what he calls a “**T-shaped leader**”.
