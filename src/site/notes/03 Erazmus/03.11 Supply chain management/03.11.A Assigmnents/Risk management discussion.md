That is a classic "Real World vs. Textbook" debate. You’ve hit on a fundamental truth of business: Resources are finite. If a small bakery spends $50,000 a year on a sophisticated "proactive risk management suite" to predict flour price fluctuations, they’ll go out of business just trying to protect themselves. In that sense, you are absolutely right—risk management must be weighed against the cost of implementation.
However, as your "teacher" for the day, I’d offer a slight correction: It’s not that some companies don’t need to be proactive; it’s that the scale and complexity of their proactivity should match their exposure.

# The "Umbrella" Principle
Think of risk management like checking the weather.
 * The Massive Corporation: They are a giant cruise ship. They need radar, satellite imaging, and a team of meteorologists because turning a ship takes miles. They must be highly proactive.
 * The Small Startup: They are a person walking to the store. They just need to look out the window. If it looks cloudy, they grab an umbrella. That "glance out the window" is still proactive risk management—it’s just cheap and fast.

# When "Reactive" Costs More Than "Proactive"
The danger in avoiding a proactive system is the "Hidden Debt" of Risk. While a system costs money today, a disaster costs significantly more tomorrow.

| Factor | Proactive Approach | Reactive Approach |
|---|---|---|
| Cost | Consistent, predictable "insurance" style spending. | $0 until a crisis, then potentially bankruptcy-level costs. |
| Reputation | High trust; you seen prepared. | Low trust; you look like you're "putting out fires." |
| Opportunity | You can take bigger risks because you have a safety net. | You are often too afraid to move forward. |

# The Sweet Spot (The "Why")
Companies shouldn't ask "Should we be proactive?" but rather "What is the minimum level of proactivity required to stay alive?" * Regulated Industries: If you’re in banking or healthcare, you don't have a choice. The "benefit" of a system is not going to jail or losing your license.
 * Low-Margin Businesses: If you operate on 2% margins, a single "unforeseen" risk (like a broken delivery truck) could wipe out your yearly profit. Here, proactivity isn't a luxury; it's survival.

# The Verdict
A "one-size-fits-all" expensive system is a waste of money for many. But every company needs a proactive mindset. Even if that "system" is just a simple checklist and a rainy-day fund, being ahead of the curve is almost always cheaper than being behind it.
