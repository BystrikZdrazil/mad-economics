Change management is a research field, and there are a lot of theories related to it. The ones we draw on hee are therefore only a small glimpse into a very large field.

*Why is it such a big field?*
Because it is difficult to create change - and therefore many have tried to crack that nut.

As with all other theories in SOM (*and other subjects*), there is **no single ultimate truth** or theory. It is about finding the approach that makes the (*most*) sense for the given company/situation/leader.

# Culture of change
An organisation’s **culture can** either **hinder** or **promote** change management.

Companies with a **strong culture of change** (*think innovative companies, perhaps with a flatter structure*) are often **better able to understand** the need for **change** and thereby enable the change itself.

Therefore, many change management theories also focus on elements such as **culture** and **readiness for change**, as these factors are crucial to whether a change management vision can succeed.

# Resistance to change
Changes in a company affect both customers/consumers and employees, and therefore resistance may arise from both groups.

**Internal resistance**: employees, management, owners
- Resistance may arise due to job (*in*)security, new processes, an unclear understanding of the purpose, as well as the process itself.

**External resistance**: customers/consumers, interest groups, politicians, etc.
- Resistance may arise due to changes in consumption habits, adaptation to new products/services, legal requirements, environmental regulations, etc.

## Education and communication
- Should create **understanding of the need for change**. The downside may be that it can take longer to achieve results. Requires **mutual trust between the parties**.

## Participation and involvement
- There is often greater support for the change **if the parties** involved **participate** in its **development**.
- CIBYIN research project

## Facilitation and support
- May, for example, include **training** or **counselling**.

## Negotiation and agreement
- **Incentives and rewards**. Particularly useful when the change creates resistance because it is perceived as a loss of something (*job security, salary level, control, etc.*). However, it may be **impractical** if it has to be done **every time a change is implemented**.

## Manipulation
- When attempting to influence opponents by *“leaking”* specific information or by *“bribing”* managers with rewards or status. Generally associated with being on the edge of ethical boundaries.

## Explicit and implicit coercion
- The **use of threats**. Can generally be **effective in the short term**, but is **unlikely to lead to good results** in the **long term**.

# Implementation and change strategies
In general, change processes can be viewed as being driven either **top-down** or **bottom-up**.

## Top-down
- Changes in strategy are **centralised decisions**.
- If the strategies can be implemented effectively, they will often be both popular and viable
- This approach is attractive to **strategic leaders** who are more **analytically focused** than focused on the behavioural aspects

## Bottom-up
- Desires for strategic direction are formed based on the **wishes of users/employees/customers** and are then **translated by management** into a strategic **direction**.

# Nudging
Nudging means *“a gentle encouragement toward a specific desirable behaviour, without removing the freedom of choice.”*

**In other words**: How do we get employees/customers/stakeholders to change behaviour or to choose or opt out of something without explicitly telling them that they *MUST*?

> [!Example]
> - How can we get more people to use public transportation instead of driving?
> - How can we influence a larger proportion of Danes’ diets to be plant-based?

Nudging is **more subtle and less noticeable** – it may even be a combination of top-down and bottom-up. Someone has decided what they want to change (*top-down*), but the actual change must come from the bottom-up.

Nudging is particularly **useful** when seeking to **influence consumers/customers**. They cannot be *“forced”* to change in the same way that internal stakeholders can.

Instead, nudging is about the company influencing consumers to choose the new alternative of their own free will.
