# Directive 2006/114/ec — misleading and comparative advertising
- Harmonises eu rules to prevent misleading advertising
- Ensures fair competition between traders
- Protects traders from unfair comparative advertising
- Supports functioning of the internal market by reducing legal fragmentation
- Why it exists:
    - Member states had widely differing laws on misleading advertising
    - Cross‑border advertising required uniform standards
    - Misleading and unlawful comparative advertising distorts competition
    - Advertising influences economic welfare of consumers and traders
    - Comparative advertising can benefit consumers if objective and fair

## Article 2 → key definitions
- **Advertising** — any representation promoting goods/services in trade
- **Misleading advertising** — deceives or is likely to deceive and affects economic behaviour or injures a competitor
- **Comparative advertising** — identifies a competitor or their goods/services explicitly or implicitly
- **Trader** — any natural/legal person acting for business purposes
- **Code owner** — entity responsible for codes of conduct

## Article 3 → criteria for misleading advertising
- Assessment considers **all features** of the advertisement
- Misleading if it deceives about:
    - Availability, nature, composition, method/date of manufacture
    - Fitness for purpose, uses, quantity, specification
    - Geographical/commercial origin
    - Results expected from use
    - Results of tests/checks
    - Price or price calculation
    - Conditions of supply
    - Advertiser’s identity, qualifications, rights, awards

## Article 4 → conditions for lawful comparative advertising
- Must not be misleading
- Must compare goods/services meeting the **same needs or purpose**
- Must compare **objectively** on material, relevant, verifiable, representative features
- Must not discredit or denigrate competitor trademarks, names, goods, services, activities
- Must not take unfair advantage of competitor reputation
- Must not present goods as imitations or replicas
- Must not create confusion between advertiser and competitor
- For products with designation of origin → comparison must be between products with the same designation

>[!important]+
>All conditions are cumulative — **must all be met**

## Articles 5 - 7 → enforcement mechanisms
- Member states must provide **adequate and effective means** to combat misleading/unlawful comparative advertising
- Organisations with **legitimate interest** may:
    - Take legal action
    - Bring cases before administrative authorities
- **Courts/authorities** may:
    - Order cessation
    - Prohibit imminent publication
    - Act without proof of actual damage or negligence
    - Require publication of corrective statements
- Advertisers may be required to **prove accuracy** of factual claims
- If evidence is not provided → claims may be deemed inaccurate

## Article 8 → member state flexibility
- May adopt stricter rules on misleading advertising
- May not adopt stricter rules on comparative advertising conditions
- May maintain advertising bans for certain goods/services
- Bans may apply to specific media

# ICC advertising and marketing communications code (2024)
- **Icc** → international chamber of commerce
- Icc advertising and marketing communications code → global self‑regulatory standard for ethical advertising
    - Complements legal frameworks
    - Promotes consumer trust and fair competition
    - Reduces need for government regulation
    - Updated regularly to reflect technological and social changes
- Core **ethical principles**:
    - Marketing communications must be legal, decent, honest, truthful
    - Must be prepared with **social responsibility**
    - Must respect **fair competition**
    - Must not abuse consumer trust or exploit lack of knowledge

## Scope of application
- Applies to **all marketing communications**, including:
    - Advertising
    - Promotions
    - Sponsorships
    - Direct marketing
    - Data‑driven marketing
    - Digital interactive media
    - Influencer content
    - Csr claims used commercially
- Does not apply to:
    - Political messaging
    - Purely informational/educational content
    - Packaging as such (but claims on packaging are covered)

## Responsibility for compliance
- Marketers hold primary responsibility
- Responsibility extends to:
    - Influencers
    - Creators
    - Affiliate networks
    - Ai systems used to generate or deliver content
    - Platforms distributing ads
- Outsourcing does not remove responsibility
- Ai‑generated content → marketer remains responsible

## Interpretation standards
- Evaluated based on **net impression**
- Assessed from viewpoint of the **reasonable consumer**
- Must consider:
    - Target group characteristics
    - Cultural and linguistic factors
    - Vulnerability (children, teens)
- Claims must be **substantiated**
- Significant updates (*2024*):
    - Expanded rules for ai and algorithmic marketing
    - New provisions on anti‑corruption, hate speech, disinformation
    - Updated environmental claims rules
    - Stronger diversity and anti‑stereotyping guidance
    - Clearer influencer marketing rules
    - New chapter on children and teens

## Key definitions
- **Claim** — any statement about product, brand, company
- **Consumer** — any person affected by marketing
- **Influencer** — individuals or ai avatars shaping audience attitudes commercially
- **Influencer marketing communications** — content conveyed by influencers
- **Direct marketing** — targeted communication using personal contact info
- **Data‑driven marketing** — marketing based on processed data

## General rules for marketing communications
- Must be clearly identifiable as marketing
- Must **not mislead** through:
    - Ambiguity
    - Exaggeration
    - Omission
- Comparisons must be fair, objective, verifiable
- Must not denigrate competitors
- Must not exploit fear, superstition, or vulnerability
- Must respect privacy and consumer preferences
- **Influencer specific rules**
    - Influencers must clearly disclose commercial relationships
    - Affiliate links count as marketing communications
    - Influencers are responsible for accuracy of claims
    - Marketers must ensure influencers comply with the code
    - Content control by marketer → triggers classification as marketing communication
- **Environmental claims**
    - Must be clear, specific, substantiated
    - Must not exaggerate environmental benefits
    - Aspirational claims must be backed by credible plans
    - Vague terms like “Eco‑friendly” require evidence
- **Children and teens**
    - Must not exploit credulity or inexperience
    - Must avoid unsafe behaviour depictions
    - Must not encourage unhealthy or irresponsible consumption
    - Must avoid pressure to purchase

# Influencer marketing
- Influencer marketing → collaboration between brands and individuals with influence
    - Uses creators’ trust and audience engagement to promote products
    - Modern evolution of celebrity endorsement
    - Relies on authenticity and niche communities
- **content marketing** — owned content to build long‑term audience
- **influencer marketing** — borrowed trust and reach
- **brand ambassadors** — long‑term advocates producing ongoing ugc

## Why influencer marketing works
- Creators build trust through parasocial relationships
- Audiences perceive creators as more authentic than brands
- Creator‑led ads often outperform traditional ads
- Discovery increasingly happens on social platforms

## Types of influencers
- Mega influencers
- Macro influencers
- Micro influencers
- Nano influencers
- Niche creators often deliver highest engagement

>[!tip]+
>Some of the main **influencer marketing channels** include _instagram, tiktok, youtube, twitch, blogs, podcasts or other social media platforms_. Influencer marketing often uses different **content types**, such as _sponsored posts, product reviews, unboxings, tutorials, livestreams, giveaways or other._

## Building an influencer campaign
1. Define goals
2. Identify target audience
3. Select influencers
4. Negotiate deliverables
5. Set kpis
6. Track performance
7. Ensure compliance with advertising rules

## Payment models
- Flat fee
- Performance‑based (*CPC, CPA*)
- Affiliate commissions
- Product gifting
- Hybrid models

## Measuring ROI
- Engagement rate
- Reach and impressions
- Conversions
- Sales lift
- Brand sentiment
- Cost efficiency of creator‑led ads
