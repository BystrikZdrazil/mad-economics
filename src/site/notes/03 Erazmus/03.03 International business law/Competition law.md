# Purpose of Competition Law
- EU economy is based on free competition and market economy
- Companies must compete on merit (price, quality, marketing)
- Competition rules ensure effective competition in the internal market
- Main rules: TFEU Articles 101 and 102
- Applies only when conduct may affect trade between Member States
- If no cross‑border effect → national law applies (e.g., Danish Competition Act)

## TFEU Article 101 - Cartel Prohibition
- Scope and definition = prohibits:
    - **Cartels** - an agreement between companies on how to sell their products
        - might take out the competition part out of international business
        - oral cartel disposition is hard to prove
    - **Agreements**, **decisions**, and **concerted practices** that **restrict competition**
        - Applies to written, oral, tacit agreements, and concerted practices
        - Concerted practice requires coordination and common will; parallel conduct alone is insufficient
- Horizontal and vertical agreements:
    - **Horizontal agreements**: between competitors at the same market level
    - **Vertical agreements**: between companies at different levels of the supply chain
- **Article 101(1)** – Prohibited Conduct = agreements with object or effect of restricting competition, including:
    - Fixing prices or trading conditions - coordinating prices
    - Limiting production, markets, technical development, or investment - controlling the production and only giving the possibility of accessing my product to certain suppliers/customers
    - Sharing markets or sources of supply
    - Applying unequal conditions to equivalent transactions
    - Forcing acceptance of unrelated supplementary obligations
- **Article 101(2)** – Legal Consequence = agreements violating 101(1) are automatically void
    - Therefore they have no legal effect
- **Article 101(3)** – Exemptions = possible if the agreement:
    - Improves production/distribution or promotes technical/economic progress
    - Gives consumers a fair share of benefits
    - Imposes no unnecessary restrictions
    - Does not eliminate competition for a substantial part of the products

## SMEs and Article 101
- SMEs (under 250 employees, turnover < €50m, balance sheet < €43m) usually fall outside article 101 because they rarely affect cross‑border trade

## De Minimis Notice (2014)
- Agreements with insignificant effect on competition are not caught by Article 101(1)
- Thresholds:
    - Horizontal agreements: combined market share < 10%
    - Vertical agreements: each party < 15%
    - If classification unclear → 10% threshold applies

## TFEU Article 102 - Abuse of Dominant Position
- **Definition of dominance** = defined by case law as a position of economic strength allowing a company to act independently of competitors, customers, and consumers 
- Market share indicators:
    - Above 50% → dominance likely
    - 40–50% → presumption of dominance if supported by other factors
    - 25–40% → usually not dominant
    - Below 25% → dominance unlikely
        - Must be stable for approximately three years
- **Abuse** - intention is irrelevant; only the effect matters 
	- Abuse includes:
	    - Imposing unfair purchase or selling prices
	    - Limiting production, markets, or technical development
	    - Applying unequal conditions to equivalent transactions
	    - Forcing unrelated supplementary obligations
- Conditions for Article 102 violation:
    - The undertaking holds a dominant position
    - The dominant position is abused
    - The abuse affects trade between Member States

# Merger Control (EU & Denmark)
- The purpose = not to prevent mergers, but to prevent creation or strengthening of dominance
    - Large mergers with EU dimension handled by the European Commission
    - Smaller mergers handled by national authorities

## Mergers
- Definition = two independent undertakings combine 
    - Acquisition of control (shares, assets, agreements)
    - Creation of a full‑function joint venture
- A merger must be notified if (**Denmark law**): 
    - Combined Danish turnover ≥ DKK 900 million and at least two parties each ≥ DKK 100 million
    - One party’s Danish turnover ≥ DKK 3.8 billion and another party’s global turnover ≥ DKK 3.8 billion
    - Referred by the Danish Business Authority (telecom mergers
- A merger is prohibited if it significantly impedes effective competition, especially by creating or strengthening dominance

# Danish Competition Act

## Purpose and Scope
- The act aims to promote efficient resource allocation through workable competition
- It applies to all commercial activity and public aid granted to commercial activity
- Anti‑competitive practices resulting directly from public regulation are exempt
- Local council practices are exempt only if necessary for legally assigned tasks
- The relevant minister decides whether a practice is exempt under public regulation
- The competition and consumer authority may issue opinions on public regulation that restricts competition
- The act does not apply to pay and working conditions (though information may be requested)
- Agreements within the same undertaking or group are exempt
- The relevant market must be defined based on demand/supply substitutability and potential competition

## Anti‑Competitive Agreements (Section 6)
- Mirrors article 101
- Prohibits agreements restricting competition
- Includes price‑fixing, market sharing, limiting production, bid‑rigging, etc.

## SME Exemption (Section 7)
- Agreements exempt if:
    - Aggregate turnover < DKK 1 billion and market share < 10%, or
    - Aggregate turnover < DKK 150 million
- Does not apply to hardcore restrictions (price‑fixing, market sharing, bid‑rigging)

## Exemptions (Section 8)
- Same criteria as article 101(3)

## Abuse of Dominance (Section 11)
- Mirrors article 102
- Prohibits abuse of dominant position
- Examples identical to article 102

## Merger Control
- Notification thresholds
- Approval, prohibition, commitments
- Deadlines: phase I (25 days), phase II (90 days)

# Dawn Raids (Inspections)
- Inspections of cartels
    - Dawn rain = ‘wait for dawn until you attack’ - they wait and then one day, they come and inspect everything

**Powers of Authorities**
- Enter premises, land, vehicles
- Inspect books, records, emails
- Copy physical and electronic data
- Seal rooms or documents
- Ask factual questions
- Inspect private devices used for work

**Obligations of Companies**
- Must cooperate
- Must not obstruct
- Must preserve evidence
- Must allow access to IT systems

**Rights of Companies**
- Right to legal counsel
- Right to a copy of collected documents
- Legal privilege for external lawyer correspondence
- Must answer factual questions only

**Practical Conduct During a Dawn Raid**
- Stay calm and professional
- Notify legal department immediately
- Accompany investigators at all times
- Avoid volunteering information
- Provide short, factual answers
- Document all interactions

>[!example]+
>**Lundbeck (2013) – Pay‑for‑Delay**
>- Lundbeck paid generics to delay entry of cheaper citalopram
>- Violated article 101
>- Fines: Lundbeck €93.8m; generics €52.2m
>- Agreements included lump‑sum payments, stock purchases for destruction, guaranteed profits
>
>**Ryanair / Aer Lingus (2013) – Merger Prohibited**
>- Ryanair attempted to acquire Aer Lingus
>- Would create dominance on 46 routes
>- Remedies insufficient
>- Prohibited under EU Merger Regulation
>
>**Danfoss (2011) – Price‑Fixing Cartel**
>- Cartel in refrigeration compressors (2004–2007)
>- Danfoss fined €90m
>- Involved price coordination and information exchange
>- Conduct carried out by a “rogue element” in German subsidiary
>- They claimed to have been completely of the cartel the employees were forming

# Enforcement Powers of the European Commission
**Investigative Powers**
- Sector inquiries
- Requests for information
- Interviews
- Inspections of business premises
- Inspections of private homes (with authorization)

**Penalties**
- Up to 1% turnover for procedural infringements
- Up to 10% turnover for substantive infringements
- Periodic penalty payments up to 5% daily turnover

**Decision‑Making Powers**
- Finding and ending infringement
- Interim measures
- Making commitments binding
- Declaring article 101/102 inapplicable
