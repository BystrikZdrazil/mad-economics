# Commodity price risk
- Risks naturally arise in everyday operations

> [!example]
> **Rising oil prices** are one of the most important risks for an airline

- Companies can reduce their vulnerability to price movements
	- Some companies instead choose to gamble for potentially better results
- It is about securing liquidity so the company can minimise losses

> [!example]
> There was a great manager in a steel refinery. He had a great history of purchases.
> 
> They later told him they wanted to buy only a sufficient amount of the stock, as they needed to change the company strategy.

# Hedging via inventory
- Long-term inventory to counter commodity price risk
	- It is expensive to **buy** the commodity
	- It is expensive to **store** the commodity
- It requires a large liquidity outlay
- If the company has no liquidity, they have to reach for external sources

# Hedging via vertical integration
- Integrating new processes vertically into the company
- Helps stabilise the commodity prices and battle shortages

>[!example]+
>Shops are being forced to ration lettuce, courgettes and aubergines after floods and storms ruined crops across the Mediterranean.
>
>The problem has become so serious that British wholesalers have taken the unusual step of importing produce from California, despite the high cost of transporting it by air.
>
>Morrisons has been helped by its vertically integrated model, which is unique among the ‘big four’ supermarkets. The model means it deals with many farmers directly and has its own farm, as well as food factories and abattoirs. 
>
>It is therefore not suffering from shortages in broccoli and iceberg lettuce, but has still had to introduce a cap on sales because other businesses, such as independent cafes and restaurants, have been desperately trying to snap up the vegetables in bulk.

# Hedging with contracts
- Hedging can increase or reduce a company’s profit
- We can never know, what the future will look like
	- Establishing contracts is therefore not the best option for everyone, especially if they aren’t confident in their market prediction

# Risks of forward contracts
Long term contracts negotiated between a specific buyer and seller can entail there risks:
- One party defaults
- Non-transparent pricing
- Difficult or impossible to cancel the contract

# Hedging with future contract
- An agreement to trade an asset on a future date at a price set today
- Futures are traded anonymously on a public exchange at a market-determined price and are generally very liquid
- Both buyer and seller can exit the contract along the way by reselling it at the current market price
- The market price is determined by supply and demand
- Futures contracts eliminate credit risk
- Eliminates credit risk
- Futures markets uses two mechanisms to prevent buyers and sellers from walking away
	- Margin -> money deposited in a trading account as security for trading futures
	- Marking to market -> daily settlement of gains/losses according to market price changes

# Mistakes to avoid
- Remember to **consider natural hedges**
	- Passing increased costs on to customers
	- Passing revenue losses to suppliers
- **Liquidity risk**
	- Hedging with futures means pressure on liquidity due to daily settlements
- **Basis risk**
	- When the futures contract does not match the physical asset 100%
- **Speculation**
	- Betting on market rises or rally goes against the intention of stability and predictability
