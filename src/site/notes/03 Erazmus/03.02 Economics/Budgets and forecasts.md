# Budgets vs Forecasts
**Budgets as Financial Plans**
Formal, static financial plans set annually, guiding resource allocation and performance evaluation.

**Forecasts as Dynamic Tools**
Frequently updated, reflecting current trends to enable agile andresponsive operational decisions.

**Function and Flexibility**
Budgets provide a fixed framework; forecasts offer flexibility to adapt to market changes and optimize operations.

**Value Creation Through Alignment**
Together, budgets and forecasts support strategic goals and operational excellence, driving organizational value.

## Purpose
The budget provides the foundation for:
- Evaluating and comparing different plans
- Assessing the implications of various choices (what‑if analyses)
- Investigating and explaining differences between actual and budgeted results

# The role of the budget
- Helps anticipate potential challenges, such as liquidity needs
- Links sales potential with production planning – enabling better resource allocation, investment decisions, staffing, and product/customer prioritization
- Serves as a communication tool to share short‑term goals with employees

# Elements of budgeting
Forecasting the need for four types of resources across different time horizons:
- **Flexible resources** (variable costs): raw materials, production labour
- **Medium‑term capacity resources** (fixed costs): rented facilities, vehicles, contracted staff
- **Long‑term capacity resources** (fixed costs): buildings, production equipment
- **Strategic resources** that enhance long‑term potential: R&D, employee development, maintenance, marketing

# The master budget
Consists of two main components:
- **Operating budget** – activity levels related to sales, purchasing and production
- **Financial budget** – financial outcomes of operating activities: income statement, cash flow (liquidity budget) and balance sheet

## What-if analysis
The master budget provides the foundation for what‑if analyses:
- Comparing alternative decisions
- Choosing between labour or machinery
- Entering new markets
- Increasing capacity
- Adjusting prices relative to expected sales volume

## Variance analysis
**Comparing budgeted and actual results to determine:**
- What caused the variance
- What actions should be taken going forward

**Budget and actual results reflect price and quantity:**
- Budgeted amount = standard price × budgeted quantity
- Actual amount = actual price × actual quantity

**Variance analysis explains differences by evaluating:**
- Differences between budgeted and actual prices
- Differences between budgeted and actual quantities
