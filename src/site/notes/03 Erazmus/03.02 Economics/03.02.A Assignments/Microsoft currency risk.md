# Advantages and Risks of Derivatives
Derivatives are financial contracts whose value is derived from an underlying asset (e.g., currencies, interest rates, commodities, equities). Companies like Microsoft use them primarily to manage financial risks.

## Advantages
- **Hedging against uncertainty** Derivatives allow firms to lock in prices or exchange rates, reducing exposure to volatility. This is especially important for multinational companies with global revenue streams.
- **Cost‑effective risk management** Hedging with derivatives is often cheaper than restructuring operations or holding large cash buffers.
- **Flexibility** Many derivative instruments can be tailored to specific exposures (e.g., forecasted foreign‑currency revenues).
- **Liquidity (for standardized products)** Futures and many options trade on exchanges with high liquidity, making them easy to enter and exit.

## Risks
- **Market risk** If the hedge is imperfect, losses can still occur.
- **Counterparty risk** For OTC (over‑the‑counter) derivatives like forwards, the other party may default.
- **Complexity** Misuse or misunderstanding can lead to unintended exposures.
- **Accounting and valuation challenges** Derivatives must be marked to market, which can introduce earnings volatility.

# Differences Between Forward Contracts, Futures, and Options
|Feature|Forward Contract|Futures Contract|Option Contract|
|---|---|---|---|
|**Where traded**|OTC (private agreement)|Exchange‑traded|Exchange‑traded or OTC|
|**Obligation**|Both parties _must_ transact at maturity|Both parties _must_ transact at maturity|Buyer has the _right_, not the obligation, to buy/sell|
|**Customization**|Fully customizable|Standardized|Customizable (OTC) or standardized (exchange)|
|**Counterparty risk**|Higher (no clearinghouse)|Very low (clearinghouse guarantees)|Depends on type; exchange‑traded options have low counterparty risk|
|**Use case**|Hedging specific exposures|Hedging broad exposures; speculation|Hedging with flexibility; speculation|

# Which Countries/Currencies Microsoft Is Most Exposed To
Microsoft earns a large share of its revenue internationally, so foreign‑exchange movements affect its reported USD results. According to Microsoft’s market‑risk disclosures:
- Microsoft hedges exposures in **euro (EUR)**, **Japanese yen (JPY)**, **British pound (GBP)**, and **Canadian dollar (CAD)**.
- These currencies therefore represent the major foreign‑exchange dependencies.

## Are these currencies historically stable?
Yes - EUR, JPY, GBP, and CAD are all considered major, relatively stable global currencies. They fluctuate, but they are not prone to extreme volatility like emerging‑market currencies.

# What Tools Microsoft Uses to Manage Exchange‑Rate Risk
Microsoft explicitly states in its market‑risk section that it:
- **Monitors foreign‑currency exposures daily**
- **Uses hedges where practicable** to offset risks
- **Hedges principal currencies** including EUR, JPY, GBP, and CAD

Although the 10‑K excerpt does not list every instrument by name, Microsoft historically uses:
- **Foreign‑currency forwards**
- **Foreign‑currency options**
- **Cross‑currency swaps**

These are standard tools for large multinationals and align with the description of “hedges” used to offset exposures.
