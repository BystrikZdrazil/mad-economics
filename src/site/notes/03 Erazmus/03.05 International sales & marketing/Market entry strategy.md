- Companies can choose between three approaches when selecting an entry strategy
    - **Naïve approach** → uses the same entry mode for all foreign markets
        - Decision-maker applies the same entry mode everywhere
        - Ignores heterogeneity of foreign markets
        - Often used by inexperienced smes
    - **Pragmatic approach** → uses a workable mode for the moment, but does not explore all alternatives
        - Decision-maker chooses a workable mode for each market
        - Firms typically begin with low‑risk export modes
        - Alternative modes are only considered if the first one fails
        - Does not guarantee the best entry mode
    - **Strategic approach →** systematically evaluates all possible entry modes before choosing
        - All alternative entry modes are systematically compared
        - Aims to maximize profit contribution subject to resources, risk and non‑profit objectives
        - Represents the analytical approach emphasized in the chapter
- Decisions on entry strategies involve many trade-offs and are complex
- “**Entry mode**” refers to institutional arrangements for entering a foreign market (*“Export, intermediate and hierarchical modes”*)

# Transaction Cost Approach

## Friction and Opportunism
- Transaction is the unit of analysis
- Friction arises from opportunistic behaviour between producer and export intermediary
- Recurring conflict areas include:
    - Export intermediary’s stock size
    - Extent of technical and commercial services
    - Division of marketing costs
    - Pricing between producer, intermediary and customers
    - Commission setting for agents
- “There is always some friction between the buyer and seller in connection with market transactions”

## Opportunistic Behaviour from Export Intermediaries
- May inflate sales‑promotion costs to receive higher reimbursement
- May manipulate market information to negotiate lower ex‑works prices
- Opportunism risk increases preference for high‑control modes (*e.g., subsidiaries*)

## Opportunistic Behaviour from Producers
- Producer may switch entry mode after intermediary builds the market
- Intermediary carries significant economic risk
- Producer may use intermediary to gain market knowledge and later internalize operations

## Intermediary Counter‑Strategies
- Intermediaries can create “**Exit barriers**” for producers by:
    - Establishing personal relations with key employees
    - Creating an independent identity
    - Adding value through before‑during‑after (*bda*) services
- If offsetting investments are impossible, intermediaries may reduce risk by representing multiple producers

# Factors Influencing Entry Mode Choice

## Overview
- Entry mode choice results from interacting internal, external, desired‑mode and transaction‑specific factors
- Model aims to “Matchmake” internal strengths with external conditions, moderated by control, risk avoidance and transaction‑specific factors
- Factors with **+** push toward internalization (*hierarchical modes*)
- Factors with **–** push toward externalization (*export modes*)

## Internal Factors
- Firm size → indicates resource availability
    - Smes often choose export modes due to limited resources
    - Larger firms increasingly use hierarchical modes
- International experience → reduces uncertainty and cost of foreign operations
    - Increases likelihood of committing resources to high‑control modes
    - Firms tend to reuse previously successful entry modes
    - Experience from similar countries (*low psychic distance*) supports high‑control modes
    - Experiential knowledge is more valuable than objective knowledge
- Product / service characteristics
    - High value/weight products often exported directly
    - Low value/weight or perishable products often require local production or licensing
    - Highly technical products requiring service favour hierarchical modes
    1. **hard services** (*decoupled production/consumption*) allow standardization and lower‑control modes
    2. **soft services** require presence abroad and favour hierarchical modes
    - Strong product differentiation advantages encourage hierarchical modes to protect know‑how and brand equity

## External Factors
- **Sociocultural distance** → large perceived distance increases uncertainty
    - Firms prefer low‑commitment modes (*agents, importers, joint ventures*)
    - Psychic distance includes more than language (*religion, democracy, industrial development, etc.)*
- **Country risk** / **demand uncertainty** → high risk encourages low‑commitment export modes
    - Political and economic unpredictability increases desire for flexibility
- **Market size and growth** → large, fast‑growing markets justify high‑commitment modes (*subsidiaries, majority jvs*)
    - Small or isolated markets are better served via exporting or licensing
- **Direct and indirect trade barriers** → tariffs and quotas encourage local production or assembly
    - Local supplier preferences encourage joint ventures or contractual arrangements
    - Product regulations may require local production
- **Intensity of competition** → high competition discourages internalization
    - Firms prefer low‑commitment export modes in highly competitive markets
- **Availability of intermediaries** → few intermediaries create “Small number bargaining”
    - Increases opportunism risk
    - Encourages hierarchical modes

## Desired Mode Characteristics
- **Risk aversion** → risk‑averse decision‑makers prefer export modes or licensing
    - Joint ventures allow shared risk but require management effort
    - Low‑risk modes may limit long‑term opportunities
- **Control** → low‑commitment modes offer low control
    - Licensing and contract manufacturing require quality monitoring
    - Joint ventures limit control and may create conflict
    - Wholly owned subsidiaries offer maximum control but require high investment
- **Flexibility** → hierarchical modes are costly and inflexible
    - Export modes offer high flexibility (*e.g., terminating agent contracts*)

## Transaction‑Specific Factors
- **Tacit nature of know‑how** → tacit knowledge is difficult to articulate and contract
    - High tacitness favours hierarchical modes to enable internal transfer
    - Investment modes allow use of organizational routines and human capital

## Classification of Entry Modes
- **Export modes**:
    - Low control
    - Low risk
    - High flexibility
- **Intermediate modes**:
    - Shared control and risk
    - Split ownership
    - Includes licensing, franchising, joint ventures
- **Hierarchical modes**:
    - High control
    - High risk
    - Low flexibility
    - Includes subsidiaries and local production units

## Challenges Between Manufacturer and Export Intermediary
- **Opposing Interests**
- Stock size disagreements
- Service level expectations
- Marketing cost allocation
- Pricing conflicts
- Commission setting
- These recurring issues increase friction and opportunism risk
