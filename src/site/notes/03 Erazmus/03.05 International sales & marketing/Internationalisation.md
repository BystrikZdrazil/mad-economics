**Entry mode decision** is a critical part of an organization's **internationalization process**. This decision occurs after a company selects a foreign market but before it defines its final marketing mix (the 4 or 7 Ps).

# The Four Factors Influencing Entry Mode Choice
To make an informed choice, organizations must evaluate four sets of factors that indicate whether they should move toward **internalization** (high control) or **externalization** (low risk/cost):

**Internal Factors:** These include the company's **resource availability**, level of **international experience**, and the complexity or **differentiation** of its products.

**External Factors:** These involve the target market’s **socio-cultural distance** from the home market, **market size and growth**, **trade barriers**, and the intensity of **competition**.

**Transaction-Specific Factors:** These address whether **outsourcing** functions to an independent partner is safe and advantageous, focusing on the ease of transferring **know-how** and the risk of **opportunistic behavior**.

**Desired Mode Characteristics:** Companies must decide their preferred levels of **risk, control, and flexibility**.

# Overview of Entry Modes
The sources categorize entry modes into three main groups based on the level of involvement and control:

1.  **Export Modes (Externalization):** 
    *   **Characteristics:** Low cost, low risk, and **high flexibility**, but offer **low control** over marketing and sales.
    *   **Examples:** Direct export, indirect export, and cooperative export.
2.  **Intermediate Modes:** 
    *   **Characteristics:** A moderate approach between exporting and full ownership.
    *   **Examples:** Joint ventures, strategic alliances, franchising, licensing, and contract manufacturing.
3.  **Hierarchical Modes (Internalization):** 
    *   **Characteristics:** **High control** through ownership, but involve **high setup costs**, high risk, and **low flexibility**.
    *   **Examples:** Sales and production subsidiaries, transnational organizations, and resident sales representatives.


> [!example] Case study
> The material uses a French dessert company to illustrate the decision-making process for entering the UK market:
> 
> **Initial Plan:** The company first considered a **sales and production subsidiary** (Hierarchical).
> 
> **Evaluation:** Analysis showed the company had **low resources** and no international experience. The UK market had **intense competition** and social-cultural differences. Additionally, the company's recipes were not considered difficult to transfer to a partner.
> 
> **Final Decision:** Because the company desired **low risk and high flexibility**, the recommended entry mode was **direct export** using a UK-based agent.
