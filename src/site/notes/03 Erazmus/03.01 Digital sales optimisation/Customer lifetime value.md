The thinking around lifecycle and CLV is complex, as different customers interact with different brands through different channels 24/7/365.

Different product types have different customer lifecycles, They vary in length and complexity e.g. fast moving consumer goods vs long-term investments, subscriptions, SaaS, etc.

Lifecycle marketing uses the customer journey and customer data to estimate touch points, purchase frequency, expected "*lifetime value*", etc.

This is relevant both in terms of generating new customers and retaining the current ones.

>[!important]+
>Remember it costs 5-25 times more to recruit a new customer than retain an existing one.

**Customere lifetime value (*CLV*)** is an estimate of total earnings of the customer relationship over its entire lifetime.

CLV can be used by all types of businesses, retail, travel agencies, publishing, software services or products with subscription and/or recurring revenue (*insurance*)

Generating new customers is a cost (*investment*) -> e.g. PPC ad, a TV campaign, promoted SoMe post, etc. = **Customer acquisition costs (*CAC*)**

# Advantages of customer lifetime value
- CLV provide clarity on customer acquisition and retention costs
- **Targeted marketing**: CLV helps allocate resources to attract and retain high-value customers effectively
- **Profit maximization**: by identifying profitable customer segments, companies can maximize overall profitability
- **Customer retention**: it emphasizes the importance of retaining existing customers, often more cost-effective than acquiring new ones
- **Data-driven insights**: CLV provides insights for product development, pricing strategies and customer service enhancements

>[!important]+
>CLV = the total amount of money a business expects to earn from a single customer over the entire relationship
>- How much did we earn from this customer today VS how much is this customer worth to us over time?

Ways to calculate:
- CLV=Avg. Purchase Value × Purchase Frequency × Customer Lifespan

>[!example]+
>–**Average purchase** = €50
>–**Customer buys** = 4 times per year
>–**Customer stays** = 3 years
>–**CLV** = 50 × 4 × 3 = €600

The **simple CLV calculation** is intuitively fine, but **ignores** things like **margins**, **churn** and **time**.

## CLV with profit margins
- (average purchase value * purchase frequency) * margin * customer lifespan.

>[!example]+
>**Average purchase** = 50€
>**Frequency** = 4/year
>**Margin** = 30%
>**Lifespan** = 3 years
>**CLV** = 50x4x0.3x3 = 180€
>
>The 600€ Revenue looked great earlier—but profit is €180, which may change some decisions

## CLV with churn rate
- For subscription or repeat businesses, churn is key
- Churn = % of customers who leave over a specific period

$$CLV = \frac{\text{Average Revenue per User} \times \text{Margin}}{\text{Churn Rate}}$$

>[!example]+
>**ARPU** = €20/month
>**Margin** = 40%
>**Churn** = 5% (0.05)
>**CLV** = (20 × 0.4) / 0.05 = €160
>
>If churn drops from 5% -> 4%, CLV jumps significantly -> Small retention improvements = big profit impact

# Various CLV models
Think of CLV as a spectrum
- **Simple CLV** = Helps you understand the concept
- **Advanced CLV** = Helps you make better business decisions

| Level        | Model          | When to use                  |
| ------------ | -------------- | ---------------------------- |
| Beginner     | Basic CLV      | Teaching, quick estimates    |
| Intermediate | Margin + churn | Most marketing decisions     |
| Advanced     | Discounted CLV | Finance / long-term planning |
| Expert       | Predictive CLV | Data-driven companies        |

>[!banana]+ Be careful
> **More advanced ≠ always better**.
>- If your **data is poor** → simple models are safer
>- If you're a **startup** → don’t overcomplicate
>- If you're **scaling** → advanced CLV becomes critical

# CLV helps you answer questions
1. How much can we spend on acquiring a new customer?
	- If a customer is worth 600€, spending 100€ to acquire them might be perfectly fine
2. Which customers are most valuable
	- Not all customers are equal, some buy more often, stay lnger, or spend more, require less
3. Should we focus on acquisition or retention?
	- CLV often reveals that retention is more profitable than acquisition
4. How to know your customers?
	- CLV provides valuable insights for product development, pricing strategies, and customer service improvements

**Things to avoid**:
- Only looking at first purchase value  
	- Ignores long-term impact
- Ignoring churn rate  
	- Customers don’t stay forever
- Treating all customers the same  
	- Segmentation is key
- Forgetting costs  
	- Real CLV should consider profit, not just revenue

# CLV challenges
- 3 basic questions about CLV
	1. Are you paying enough to acquire customers through channels and platforms?
	2. Are you getting hold of the "*right*" customers?
	3. How much can you spend on acquiring and retaining them

## How to increase CLV
1. Improve customer experience
	- Fast and personal customer service
	- Easily navigable websites and user-friendly apps
2. Increase the frequency of purchases
	- Email marketing and retargeting ads that remind customers to buy again
	- Subscription models and loyalty programs
3. Increase average order value
	- Upselling: Sell more expensive versions of a product
	- Cross-selling: Offer related products at checkout
4. Reduce churn rate
	- Focus on customer satisfaction and engagement
	- Send personalized offers to customers who are churning

# CLV:CAC ratio
The smartest ways to reduce CAC in 2026
- Target high-value customers
	- Cheap customers are not always the best customers
- Use paid ads to learn, then scale what works
	- Ads reveal which messages, audiences and offers resonate
- Build owned channels
	- Email
	- SEO
	- Content
	- Community
	- Referrals
- Fix conversion leaks first
	- Don't pour more money into into a broken funnel
- Always compare CAC with CLV
	- A "*high*" CAC can be find of the customer is very valuable

>[!example]+
>A fashion webshop used 5000€ on campaigns
>- At first glance, TikTok looks good, because CAC is only 20€, but google search may be more profitable because those customers have much higher CLV
>- The smart marketeer would not simply cut Google, they would ask "*Which channel gives us the best long-term profit per customer?*"
>
>The real goal: **not the lowest CAC, but the most profitable CAC**

# The smartest ways to reduce CAC
Make every step from **targeting** -> click -> conversion -> repeat purchase **more efficient**

>[!example]+
>1. Improve conversion rate before buying more traffic
>2. Target better-fit audiences, not just bigger audiences
>3. Use organic and owned channels to reduce dependency on paid ads
>4. Improve creative testing
>5. Retarget ”warm audiences” carefully (*audience that already knows you*)
>6. Strengthen the offer
>7. Improve sales and lead qualification
>8. Reduce churn and increase CLV
>9. Cut waste with better measurement

## Improve CRO before buying more traffic
| **Scenario** | **Ad spend** | **Website visitors** | **Conversion rate** | **Customers** | **CAC** |
| ------------ | ------------ | -------------------- | ------------------- | ------------- | ------- |
| *Before*       | €1,000       | 2,000                | 1%                  | 20            | €50     |
| *After CRO*    | €1,000       | 2,000                | 2%                  | 40            | €25     |

Same budget. Double the customers. CAC cut in half.

Smart actions:
- Improve landing page speed
- Match the ad message to the landing page
- Make the call-to-action obvious
- Remove unnecessary form fields
- Add reviews, testimonials, guarantees, other "*trust signals*", etc.
- Google also emphasizes landing page experience, ad relevance, ad strength and ad assets as ways to improve ad performance. Better relevance can improve click-through and conversion efficiency.
