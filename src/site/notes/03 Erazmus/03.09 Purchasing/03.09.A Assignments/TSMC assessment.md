# List of products and services
## 1. Direct Materials (*Raw Ingredients*)
These are the physical materials consumed during the chip-making process.

**Silicon Wafers**
The "*canvas*" for chips. TSMC sources these in various sizes (*primarily 300mm/12-inch*) from suppliers like **Shin-Etsu Handotai**, **SUMCO**, and **GlobalWafers**.

**Specialty Chemicals & Gases**
High-purity chemicals for cleaning, etching, and developing. Key providers include **Air Liquide** (*gases*), **BASF**, and **Merck/Entegris**.

**Photoresists**
Light-sensitive chemicals used in lithography to "*print*" circuit patterns.

**Precious Metals**
Gold, copper, and cobalt used for the microscopic wiring (*interconnects*) between transistors.

**Mask Blanks**
Highly specialized glass plates used to create the templates (*photomasks*) for chip designs.

## 2. Manufacturing Equipment (*The Tools*)
TSMC spends tens of billions of dollars annually (*CapEx*) on the world's most advanced machinery.

**Lithography Systems**
The most critical (*and expensive*) tools. TSMC is the largest customer of **ASML** for Extreme Ultraviolet (*EUV*) and Deep Ultraviolet (*DUV*) machines.

**Etch & Deposition**
Tools that carve patterns and deposit thin films of material. Major suppliers include **Applied Materials**, **Lam Research**, and **Tokyo Electron** (*TEL*).

**Metrology & Inspection**
Machines that scan for defects at the atomic level, provided by **KLA Corporation** and **Applied Materials**.

**Cleaning Tools**
Specialized equipment to ensure wafers remain 100% contaminant-free.

## 3. Design & Software Ecosystem (*The Brains*)
**EDA Tools** (*Electronic Design Automation*)
Sophisticated software used by engineers to design circuits. TSMC partners closely with **Cadence**, **Synopsys**, and **Siemens EDA**.

**IP** (*Intellectual Property*)
Pre-designed circuit blocks (*like CPU cores or USB controllers*) from partners like **ARM**, which are integrated into the manufacturing process.

**Cloud Computing**
Massive server capacity for R&D and design simulations, often utilizing **AWS**, **Microsoft Azure**, or **Google Cloud**.

## 4. Facilities & Construction
**Cleanroom Engineering**
Specialized firms like **United Integrated Services** (*UIS*) design and build the ultra-clean environments where chips are made.

**HVAC & Air Filtration**
Massive systems to keep air quality thousands of times cleaner than a hospital operating room.

**Utilities**
TSMC is a massive consumer of **electricity** (*increasingly shifting to renewable energy via PPA agreements*) and **ultra-pure water** (*UPW*)

## 5. Backend & Logistics
Once the wafers are finished, they need to be tested, cut, and shipped.

**Testing Equipment**
High-speed testers from **Advantest** and **Teradyne** to ensure every chip works.

**OSAT** (*Outsourced Semiconductor Assembly and Test*)
While TSMC does much of its own advanced packaging (*like CoWoS*), it also uses partners like **ASE Group** and **Amkor** for traditional packaging.

**Specialized Logistics**
Secure, vibration-controlled transportation for delicate wafers and multi-ton lithography machines.

# Differentiation
## By category
| **Category**                | **Examples**                                          | **Primary Objective**                              |
| --------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------- |
| *Capital Equipment (CapEx)* | EUV Lithography, Etching tools, Metrology systems     | Technological leadership & capacity expansion.     |
| *Direct Materials*          | Silicon wafers, Process gases, Photoresists, Slurries | Continuous, high-yield production.                 |
| *Indirect Materials (MRO)*  | Spare parts, Cleanroom suits, Lubricants, Filters     | Operational maintenance and uptime.                |
| *Design & IP Services*      | EDA software (Cadence), ARM processor architectures   | Enabling customers to design chips for TSMC nodes. |

## By complexity
### Strategic Items (*High Risk, High Impact*)
- **EUV Lithography Machines:** There is only one supplier in the world (*ASML*). The complexity is infinite, and the impact is existential.
- **Advanced Packaging Materials:** Specialized substrates for "*Chiplets*" (*CoWoS*) are currently a major bottleneck.

### Bottleneck Items (*High Risk, Low Impact*)
- **Rare Process Gases:** For example, **Neon** or **Xenon**. They don't cost as much as a machine, but geopolitical shifts (*like the war in Ukraine*) can make them nearly impossible to acquire, halting production.

### Leverage Items (*Low Risk, High Impact*)
- **Standard Silicon Wafers:** While critical, there are multiple high-quality global suppliers. TSMC uses its massive buying power to negotiate favourable terms.

### Non-Critical/Routine Items (*Low Risk, Low Impact*)
- **Standard Office/IT supplies:** Important for the business, but easily substituted and managed through automated procurement.

# Differences between procurement methods
## Raw ingredients
This is being supplied on a regular basis, where the company receives recurring batches of materials needed for non-stop production

## Manufacturing equipment
The manufacturing equipment in this field is bought only as-needed and being used for a long period of time afterwards, as the machines are extremely expensive (*150M - 400M USD*)

They also use smaller tools, for example to purify the air and keep the environment sterile, clothes for the workers and other daily usage tools. These are bought as-needed for the ones that are meant to last and recurringly for consumables (*respirators for the employees*)

## Design & software ecosystem
This is being constantly developed, with the developers constantly adding new features and improving the user experience.

## Facilities & construction
Facilities are also bought as-needed, usually when the company wants to expand their production. However, to my knowledge there haven't been any expansions in the recent times.

## Backend & Logistics
TSMC has a close collaboration with their logistic partners, ensuring that the products get to their designated locations on time and safely. While they do most of their own packaging, they also utilise some companies to produce it for them.

# Value driven
TSMC is definitely value driven, as it offers some of the most advanced production in the world. They need to focus on how to manufacture all the orders they receive in time and how to make it affordable for their customers, so they can still sell it later and make a profit from it.

# How logistical efficiency can be improved by the company
TSMC's logistics can be improved by deepening its global geographic diversification, accelerating automation within its fabs, implementing AI-driven supply chain visibility tools, and strengthening green logistics practices. As the world's largest chipmaker expands its "*gigafab*" clusters across the U.S., Europe, and Asia, enhancing supply chain resilience while maintaining high-yield efficiency is critical.
