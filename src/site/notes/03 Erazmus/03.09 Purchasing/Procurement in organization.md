# Centralized VS decentralized
Depends on:
- Organization **size**
	- **Ownership** (*with certain companies, especially family businesses, they want to have it decentralized to keep it simple*)
- Regionally on **culture**
- **Product**
- Procurement strategy
- Supplier situation

|**Centralized Purchasing**|**Decentralized Purchasing**|
|---|---|
| **The function is of great strategic importance**: High-level oversight ensures alignment with company goals.| **Dynamic market with short product life**: Allows for faster, local responses to rapid market changes.|
| **Economies of scale can be achieved**: Bulk buying power is maximized through a single point of contact.| **The market is competitive**: Local units can negotiate more flexibly with regional vendors.|
| **Ongoing internal considerations regarding strategic initiatives**: Central control maintains consistency across the brand.| **Collaboration between customers and suppliers in the supply chain is optimized**: Local proximity improves relationship management.|
| **Senior management has laid the line around the purchasing function's activities, limitations and opportunities**: Standardized policies are set from the top.| **JIT production and focus on optimization of the value chain**: Local control allows for "Just-In-Time" agility and reduced lead times.|
| **Low complexity of procurement**: Simpler items often don't require the overhead of a central department.|

# Organizational placement
|**Strategic Importance**|**Organizational Placement**|**Function**|
|---|---|---|
|*High*|Positioned as a line function or as a staff function reporting directly to top management|Purchasing is involved in strategic decision-making related to the company's overall strategy. The CPM (Chief Purchasing Manager) participates in the executive management team. The purchasing function serves the entire organization.|
|*Medium*|Positioned as a separate function, e.g. within the logistics department or the finance function|Purchasing activities are typically not directly included in the company's strategic considerations, and purchasing is therefore not represented in the executive management team. Tasks performed are typically related to the logistics function or cover parts of the company's purchasing of indirect goods as part of the finance|
|*Low*|Positioned as a department under e.g. production or warehousing|Purchasing is an operational function, often placed with direct reference to the function that has the need to purchase. More strategic purchasing tasks are often handled by functional managers, while the purchasing function's tasks are, for example, to order goods based on production or inventory requirements.|

# 3 levels of strategy in an organisation
1. Corporate
	- All-embracing, linking the business together
2. Business
	- Concerned with brand issues, such as how to compete in different markets (*home or overseas*)
3. Operational
	- Concerned with functional activities such as marketing, purchasing, production or finance

# Developing an effective strategy
**Defining Clear Objectives**
Setting clear goals like growth, profitability, and sustainability is the first step in effective strategy development.

**Making Strategic Trade-Offs**
Choosing among alternatives and making trade-offs is essential, as pursuing all opportunities simultaneously is not practical.

**Balancing Innovation and Risk**
Incorporating innovation while managing risks ensures strategy adapts to uncertainty and long-term challenges.

**Aligning Procurement Strategy**
Procurement strategies balance cost, quality, supplier choices, and sustainability to support overall business goals.

# Strategy in organizational context
**Definition of Strategy**
Strategy integrates an organization’s goals, policies, and actions into a cohesive plan providing clear direction.

**Role in Resource Allocation**
Strategy enables effective resource allocation based on internal strengths and external opportunities.

**Strategic Clarity in Procurement**
Strategic clarity aligns sourcing, supplier relations, and cost management with long-term organizational goals.

**Importance of Management Vision**
Management vision shapes strategy by defining organizational identity and future direction.

# Importance of strategic analysis
**Foundation for Decision-Making**
Strategic analysis provides essential insights into organizational position and environment for informed decisions.

**Role in Supply Chain Management**
It helps assess supplier markets, costs, risks, and competitive pressures in procurement and supply chains.

**Supports Strategic Options Evaluation**
Strategic analysis clarifies assumptions and highlights consequences of different strategic choices.

**Reduces Uncertainty and Aligns Strategy**
Systematic analysis reduces uncertainty and aligns procurement strategies with overall business objectives.

# Analysis tools
**STEEPLE Analysis Overview**
STEEPLE analysis examines social, technological, economic, environmental, political, legal, ethical, and demographic factors impacting organizations.

**[[03 Erazmus/03.05 International sales & marketing/Porters analysis\|Porter’s Five Forces Model]]**
Porter’s Five Forces model analyzes competitive forces like supplier power, buyer power, and industry rivalry.

**Product Life Cycle and Portfolio Tools**
Product Life Cycle and portfolio analysis tools help evaluate product maturity and balance investments across categories.

**SWOT Analysis**
SWOT analysis evaluates internal strengths and weaknesses alongside external opportunities and threats for strategic fit.
