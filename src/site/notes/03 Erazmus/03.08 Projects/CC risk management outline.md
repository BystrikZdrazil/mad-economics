# Commodities risk management
As a leading manufacturer of balers and compactors, **Bramidan** is highly sensitive to fluctuations in the industrial commodity markets. Because their products are essentially heavy mechanical structures designed to compress waste using high-pressure hydraulics, their cost base is dominated by raw materials rather than complex electronics.

Below are the primary commodity price risks for Bramidan in the current 2026 economic landscape.

### Steel (Hot-Rolled Coil and Plate)
Steel is the most critical commodity for Bramidan. It constitutes approximately **65% to 90%** of the weight of a vertical baler or compactor.

- **Why it matters:** Steel is the primary driver of the **Cost of Goods Sold (COGS)**. Any significant price hike directly erodes profit margins unless Bramidan can pass these costs to customers through price surcharges, which is difficult in competitive tender-based markets.
- **Probability of Volatility:** **High.** While 2026 forecasts suggest a slight stabilization in European steel prices (averaging around **€575–€600/tonne**), the full implementation of the **EU’s Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM)** adds a "green premium" that creates upward pressure on local prices.
- **Scope of Impact:** **Critical.** A **10%** increase in steel prices can lead to a **4–6%** increase in the total manufacturing cost of a machine.

### Hydraulic Oil and Mineral Base Oils
Bramidan’s machines rely on hydraulic cylinders to generate the pressure needed for compaction.
- **Why it matters:** Hydraulic oil is a consumable that Bramidan must include with every new machine and use for ongoing maintenance services. Prices are closely tied to crude oil benchmarks (Brent/WTI).
- **Probability of Volatility:** **Medium.** Brent crude is projected to hover around **$60–$70/barrel** in 2026 due to an oversupply in the global market. However, geopolitical tensions in the Middle East or Eastern Europe remain "wildcard" factors that can cause sudden spikes.
- **Scope of Impact:** **Moderate.** While the volume per machine is significant (tens of liters), it represents a smaller percentage of the total machine value compared to steel. The impact is felt more in the **Service & Maintenance** division.

### Industrial Electricity (Denmark Production)
Bramidan produces its machines in Denmark, a country with some of the highest industrial electricity taxes and levies in the EU.
- **Why it matters:** Manufacturing processes like **laser cutting, welding, and powder coating** are energy-intensive. High energy costs increase the "per-unit" overhead of every machine built in the Danish facility.
- **Probability of Volatility:** **Medium-High.** Danish wholesale prices are highly dependent on wind availability and regional gas prices. 2025 saw **17%** price increases in some zones; while 2026 futures are more stable, winter "calm periods" (Dunkelflaute) can cause extreme short-term spikes.
- **Scope of Impact:** **Low-Medium.** Energy is a smaller component of the total cost than raw materials, but it affects the **competitiveness** of Danish production versus manufacturers in lower-energy-cost regions like Eastern Europe or Asia.

### Marine and Road Freight (Fuel-Linked)
Since Bramidan exports to over 40 countries, the cost of moving heavy steel machines is a major variable.
- **Why it matters:** Logistics costs are heavily influenced by **Diesel and Marine Gas Oil (MGO)** prices. Because balers are heavy and bulky, they are expensive to ship; a rise in fuel prices increases the "Landed Cost" for international distributors.
- **Probability of Volatility:** **Medium.** Global freight markets in 2026 are adjusting to new emissions regulations (EU ETS for shipping). While fuel prices are expected to be soft, "green" surcharges are becoming a permanent fixture in shipping contracts.
- **Scope of Impact:** **Medium.** High freight costs can make Bramidan’s products less competitive in distant markets like North America or Australia compared to local manufacturers.

### Systemic Risk
- **Source**: Macroeconomic Uncertainties (Inflation & Fiscal Consolidation)
- **Impact**: On the general economy
- **Bramidan Context** (*Slovakia*): Slovakia is currently facing a period of high inflation (projected at roughly 4.1% for 2026) and rigorous fiscal consolidation to reduce public deficits. For Bramidan, this systemic risk manifests as a general slowdown in private investment across Slovakia. When the general economy is suppressed by government spending cuts and high costs of living, businesses in all sectors are less likely to invest in new capital equipment like waste balers.

### Market Risk
- **Source**: Regulatory or Institutional Uncertainties
- **Impact**: On the waste management and manufacturing sector
- **Bramidan Context** (*EU/Global*): The EU Packaging and Packaging Waste Regulation (PPWR) is set to apply from August 2026. This creates a significant "regulatory uncertainty." While it may increase demand for recycling equipment, it also forces Bramidan to ensure their machines are compatible with new, strictly defined "recyclable packaging" types. Failure to adapt machine specifications to meet these specific EU-wide standards could lead to a loss of market share to more agile competitors.

### Operational Risk
- **Source**: Supply Uncertainties (Raw Materials & Specialized Components)
- **Impact**: On Bramidan’s specific supply chain
- **Bramidan Context**: Bramidan’s machines rely heavily on steel and specialized hydraulic fluids. Furthermore, their newer "intelligent" models (BRA-IN software) require electronic sensors. An operational risk exists in the potential for supply chain delays of these critical components. Given the current global trade tensions and logistics challenges for "non-standard" heavy freight (as balers are bulky), any failure in the timely delivery of hydraulic systems or chips would halt the production line in Denmark or disrupt deliveries to Slovakian distributors.

### Credit and Liquidity Risk
- **Source**: Uncertainty about the payment cycle
- **Impact**: On specific supply chain nodes (B2B Clients)
- **Bramidan Context** (*Slovakia*): Bramidan deals extensively with SMEs in the retail and logistics sectors in Slovakia. As noted in your table, these segments often suffer from greater information asymmetry. If Slovakian retail partners experience a cash flow "crunch" due to high energy prices or the 2026 VAT hikes, they may seek to extend their payment cycles. This creates a liquidity risk for Bramidan, as delayed payments from clients can cause a mismatch in the firm’s short-term financial commitments to its own suppliers.
