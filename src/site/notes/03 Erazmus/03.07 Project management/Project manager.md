# What does a project manager do?
- Manages the people of a team
- Schedules the timeline
- Talks to the board/superiors/contractors
- Gathers knowledge
- Makes key decisions

# Who is a Project Manager?
- Project Manager - a person in charge of a project - should (be):
    - Helps lead the project towards success while aligning different parts and aspects of the project - controlling and organizing - **budget management**
    - Coordinates a team of different specialities in order to achieve a goal = ensure **teamwork**
    - Sets responsibilities for different members of the team = **milestones and goals**
    - Makes sure that deadlines are met = **time-management and supervising**
        - Multitasking + predicting and setting initial timelines
    - Motivates
    - Negotiates deals
    - Manages people
        - Informing important stakeholders and ensuring good knowledge of problems = **well-rounded**
    - Strong and independent leader + flexible = being able to **adapt**
        - Information gathering + decision making
    - Structured and challenging work
    - Overview over things - SWOT analysis
        - Risk management
    - Delegates tasks - who? when? what?
    - communicative and sympathetic personality + structured and assertive
        - Handling conflicts
    - Emotionally intelligent + analytical
    - Settle & ensure good and comfortable work culture

## Core Responsibilities
- **Inward Leadership** - build and lead the project team
    - allocate tasks and responsibilities
    - ensure motivation and psychological safety
    - manage conflicts and maintain team cohesion
- **Outward Management** - communicate with stakeholders, steering committee, and external partners
    - negotiate resources, deadlines, and expectations
    - represent the project in the organization
- **Project Management** - ensure progress according to time, cost, and quality constraints
    - monitor risks, [[03 Erazmus/03.07 Project management/03.07.A Assignments/Project strategy Nordisk\|adjust plans]], and maintain documentation
    - facilitate decision-making and maintain project governance
- **Task Management** - ensure the project delivers its intended results
    - maintain focus on goals and deliverables
    - coordinate workflows and dependencies

## Complexity of the Role
- Must balance people, tasks, organization, and external environment
- Often works in a matrix structure → dual reporting lines (line manager vs. project owner)
- Success depends heavily on management support, resource allocation, and organizational hierarchy

# Theories Relevant to Project Managers

## Blake & Mouton Managerial Grid
- **Two axis**:
    - Concern for people
    - Concern for production
- Helps **identify leadership style**:
    - **Country Club** (*High people, low task*)
    - **Team Leader** (*High people, high task*) → **ideal**
    - **Impoverished** (*Low/low*)
    - **Produce-or-Perish** (*High task, low people*)
- Useful for **choosing the right approach** depending on team needs

## Belbin’s 9 Team Roles
- Effective teams require a balance of roles:
    - **Social roles**: Resource Investigator, Team worker, Coordinator
    - **Thinking roles**: Plant, Monitor Evaluator, Specialist
    - **Action roles**: Shaper, Implementer, Completer-Finisher
- Helps project managers build complementary teams and avoid role conflicts

## Hersey & Blanchard Situational Leadership
- Leadership style depends on team member’s **development level**:
    - **U1**: Low competence, high commitment
    - **U2**: Some competence, low commitment
    - **U3**: Moderate/high competence, variable commitment
    - **U4**: High competence, high commitment
- PM adapts between:
    - Directing
    - Coaching
    - Supporting
    - Delegating

## Myers-Briggs (MBTI)
- Based on 4 dimensions (e.g., E/I, S/N, T/F, J/P)
- Helps PM understand:
    - How team members **communicate**
    - How they **make decisions**
    - What **motivates** them
- Supports better collaboration and conflict management

## Adizes PAEI Model
- Four leadership roles:
    - **P – Producer** (results-oriented)
    - **A – Administrator** (structure, processes)
    - **E – Entrepreneur** (vision, innovation)
    - **I – Integrator** (team cohesion)
- **Ideal leader balances all four**, but most people have *1–2 dominant roles*
- Different project phases require different leadership strengths

# Organizational Context of the Project Manager

## Key Groups
- **Steering Committee** – highest authority, approves major decisions
- **Project Owner** – initiates project, defines goals
- **Project Group** – executes tasks
- **Decision-Making Group** – may include customer representatives
- **Advisory Group** – provides expert input
- **Reference Group** – idea testing and feedback
- **User Group** – end users of project output

- **Challenges**:
    - Conflicts between line and project responsibilities.
    - Limited resources (time, budget, people
    - Need for strong management support to ensure legitimacy

## Why Projects Fail
- Five root causes:
    - **Poor communication** → misalignment, unclear expectations, missing updates
    - **Unclear goals** / **scope creep** → no shared understanding of success criteria
    - **Inadequate planning** → missing risk analysis, unrealistic timelines
    - **Lack of stakeholder engagement** → resistance, lack of buy-in, slow decision-making
    - **Weak leadership** / **team issues** → low motivation, unclear roles, unresolved conflicts

>[!note]+
>Key takeaway = early detection of warning signs + proactive adjustments = **higher success rate**

# Future of Project Management
- **Trends shaping 2025**:
    - AI-driven project management → automation of scheduling, reporting, risk prediction
    - Hybrid methodologies → ix of Agile + Waterfall depending on project type
    - Sustainability focus → environmental and social impact integrated into project goals
    - Remote & distributed teams → requires stronger digital leadership and communication skills
    - Data-driven decision-making → PMs must interpret analytics and dashboards
- **Future skills needed**:
    - Technological fluency → understanding AI tools, automation, and digital platforms
        - Ability to integrate tech into project workflows
    - Strategic thinking → seeing the big picture, aligning projects with organizational goals
    - Emotional intelligence → managing diverse teams, empathy, conflict resolution
    - Adaptability → navigating rapid change, uncertainty, and evolving project environments

## How to Succeed as a Project Manager
- **Strong management support** and clear reporting lines
- **Access to resources** (*budget, people, equipment*)
- Ability to **motivate**, **communicate**, and **adapt**
- **Understanding team dynamics** and applying the right leadership style
- Using **data and technology** to improve decision-making
- Ensuring the project delivers both:
    - **Formal success** (*time, cost, quality*)
    - **User success** (*usable, valuable outcomes*)
