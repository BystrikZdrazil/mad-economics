# Brainstorming 
 
## What is brainstorming 
- Creative activity or business method 
- A group of people meet to suggest many new ideas for developments or solutions 
- Used during the project process 
- Definition: “A group of people meet to suggest a lot of new ideas for possible developments or solutions” 

## Types of brainstorming 
 
### Mind mapping 
- Visual approach starting with a central keyword 
- Ideas branch outward to explore connections 
 
### Starbursting 
- Generating questions instead of answers 
- Explores a topic by asking *who*, *what*, *when*, *where*, *why*, *how* 
 
### Rolestorming 
- Collecting ideas from the perspective of another person 
 
### Scamper 
- Substitute, combine, adapt, modify, put to another use, eliminate, reverse 
- Used to modify existing ideas 
 
### Reverse brainstorming 
- Instead of solving the problem, the group thinks of ways to worsen it 
- Reversed points are then used to find solutions 
 
## Brainwriting (6‑3‑5 method) 
 
### Concept 
- Silent idea‑generation technique
- **6** participants
- **3** written ideas each round
- **5** minutes per round before notes rotate
- Participants use previous notes as inspiration
- Repeated until many ideas are generated
 
### Advantages 
- Minimizes dominant personalities 
- **Motivates introverted** team members 
- Ensures **equal voice** 
- Enhances creativity 
 
### When to use 
- When a **large number of ideas is needed** 
- For **complex problems** requiring creative thinking 
- When the team is stuck in a creative lead 
 
### Tips 
- Use a shared visible timer 
- Appoint a facilitator 
- Collect all ideas in a shared document afterward 
 
# Feedback 
 
## Definition of feedback 
- Information about reactions to a product or performance used as a basis for improvement 
 
## Foundation of feedback 
- **Openness**
	- All parties should be ready to give and receive feedback
- **Reflection**
	- Feedback should be rationally presented and reflected on
- **Distance**
	- Parties should distance themselves from personal feelings
- **Active listening**
	- All parties need to actively listen to others’ needs

## Rules of feedback 
 
### Receiving 
- Listen to given feedback
- Be aware of responses (*body language, words*)
- Show openness
- Seek understanding and clarification
- Reflect or seek a second opinion
- Take action
 
### Giving 
- Use “I” messages 
- Balance positive and negative feedback 
- Be specific 
- Be realistic 
- Choose appropriate timing 
- Offer support 
 
## Preparation 
- Ensure appropriate surroundings 
- Structure the feedback 
- Ensure readiness of the receiver 
- Be ready for compromise 
 
## Feedback models 
 
### [[03 Erazmus/03.07 Project management/03.07.B Tools/Models#DESC Model\|Desc model]]
- **Describe** 
	- Explain the situation 
- **Express**
	- Express the impact on you, the team, or the organization
- **Specify**
	- Explain how the recipient should change their behavior
- **Consequences**
	- Show how the suggested actions will affect the team or organization
 
### [[03 Erazmus/03.07 Project management/03.07.B Tools/Models#CEDAR Model\|Cedar model]]
- **Context** 
	- Paint the scene and explain context 
- **Examples** 
	- Give clear examples of behavior 
- **Diagnosis** 
	- Ask for reasons behind the behavior 
- **Actions** 
	- Focus on future actions and improvements 
- **Review** 
	- Conclude feedback and ensure understanding 
 
### [[03 Erazmus/03.07 Project management/03.07.B Tools/Models#360° Model\|360 feedback]] 
- Collects feedback from many sources
- **Includes self‑evaluation**
- Often used in **annual performance reviews**
- Neutral third‑party analysis
- Very formal

# [[03 Erazmus/03.07 Project management/03.07.B Tools/Stakeholder analysis\|Stakeholder analysis]] 
 
## What is a stakeholder 
- Any person or group that can affect, be affected by, or have an interest in a project and its outcomes 
 
### Examples 
- Customer 
- Finance manager 
- Project manager 
- Journalist 
 
### Internal vs external stakeholders 
- Internal: inside the organization, involved in or affected by the project 
- External: outside the organization, influencing or affected by the project 
 
### Primary vs secondary stakeholders 
- Primary: directly affected by the project 
- Secondary: indirectly affected or influencing the project 
 
## Stakeholder analysis process 
 
### Identify stakeholders 
- List all internal and external stakeholders 
- Gather information on their interest and influence 
 
### Assess influence and interest 
- Estimate each stakeholder’s influence and interest 
 
### Categorize stakeholders 
- Place each stakeholder on the matrix 
- Review placements with the project team 
 
### Engage and monitor 
- Apply the right engagement approach 
- Track needs and influence 
- Gather feedback and adjust communication 
 
## Stakeholder matrix
- Tool that classifies stakeholders by influence and interest 
 
### Purpose 
- Supports project planning, change management, strategic execution, and organizational transformation
 
### Benefits
- Gives clear overview of stakeholder priorities
- Easy to create
- Keeps communication focused
 
### How to use 
- Identify stakeholders 
- Rate power and interest 
- Place them in the matrix 
- Choose engagement approach 
 
## Power‑interest matrix 
 
### Low power, low interest 
- Monitor 
- Avoid excessive communication 
 
### Low power, high interest 
- Keep informed 
- Maintain regular contact 
- Valuable source of input 
 
### High power, low interest 
- Keep satisfied 
- Manage carefully 
 
### High power, high interest 
- Manage closely 
- Key decision‑makers 
 
 
# Waterfall 
 
## What is waterfall 
- Traditional **linear** project management method 
- **Work flows step‑by‑step with no overlaps** 
- Best for stable, well‑defined requirements 
- Originates in construction and manufacturing 
 
## Key characteristics
- Sequential phases
- Upfront planning with **fixed scope**, **budget**, **timeline**
- Heavy **documentation**
- **Milestone‑based** progress
- **Low flexibility**
 
## Phases
- **Requirements**: gather all requirements and create specification document
- **Design**: architecture, technical design, workflows
- **Implementation**: development based on design
- **Testing**: test product against requirements
- **Deployment**: deliver final product
- **Maintenance**: fix issues, update system, support
 
## Strengths
- Predictability
- Stable budget and timeline
- Strong **documentation and control**
- Ideal for regulated industries
 
## Weaknesses 
- Low flexibility 
- **Limited customer involvement** 
- Late testing increases risk of rework 
 
## When waterfall works best 
- Requirements are clear and stable 
- Documentation is mandatory 
- Budget and timeline fixed 
- Project is **predictable or similar to past ones** 
- Industries include construction, engineering, pharmaceutical, aerospace 
 
## Waterfall vs agile 
 
| **Feature**      | **Waterfall** | **Agile**    |
| ---------------------- | ------------- | ---------------- |
| *Process*       | linear    | iterative cycles |
| *Planning*       | upfront    | evolves     |
| *Requirements*     | fixed early  | changeable    |
| *Customer involvement* | minimal    | continuous    |
 
## Modern adaptations
 
### Extended waterfall
- Adds limited feedback loops
- Allows minor adjustments
 
### V‑model
- Each phase has a corresponding testing phase
- Emphasizes **early planning for quality**
 
### Hybrid models
- **Combines** waterfall and agile
- **Waterfall** for **budgeting** and **documentation**
- **Agile** for **development** and **iterative delivery**
 
## Key takeaways 
- Structured and predictable 
- Best for stable requirements 
- Strong control and documentation 
- Not ideal for fast‑changing environments 
 
# Scrum
 
## Introduction
- Agile methods became relevant due to high uncertainty and need for flexibility
- Scrum addresses these challenges
 
## What is scrum
- Agile **project management framework**
- Mostly used in IT and development
- Focuses on **iterative development**, **cooperation**, ongoing **feedback**
 
## When scrum is used
- **Unspecified** or **changing demands**
- High **complexity**
- Need for **ongoing feedback**
- Relates to **goals and methods theory** (*type 2 and type 3 projects*)
 
## Scrum roles
 
### Scrum master 
- **Facilitates process** 
- Removes obstacles 
- Ensures correct use of scrum 
 
### Product owner 
- **Represents stakeholders** 
- Responsible for **product backlog** 
- Prioritizes tasks 
 
### Development team 
- Interdisciplinary team 
- **Delivers the product** 
 
## Scrum artifacts
- **Product backlog**
	- List of demands
- **Sprint backlog**
	- Tasks chosen for the sprint
- **Increment**
	- Result after the sprint
 
## Scrum events
- **Sprint**
	- Lasts 1–4 weeks
- **Sprint planning**
	- Meeting to decide what and how to work during the sprint
- **Daily scrum**
	- Short daily meeting to coordinate tasks
- **Sprint review**
	- Work presented to stakeholders
	- Feedback collected
- **Sprint retrospective**
	- Team reflects on sprint
	- Identifies improvements
 
## Advantages of scrum 
- High flexibility 
- Fast feedback 
- Great transparency 
- Close cooperation with stakeholders 
- Early value creation 
 
## Limitations 
- Requires **discipline and engagement** 
- Less suited for stable, predictable projects 
- Less suited for fixed‑scope contracts 
- Risk of **scope creep** 
 
 
# RACI matrix 
 
## What is the RACI matrix 
- Project management tool **defining roles** 
- Clarifies responsibilities in people and management 
 
## Meaning behind RACI letters 
- **R** responsible 
- **A** accountable 
- **C** consulted 
- **I** informed 
 
## Why RACI is useful 
- Prevents confusion 
- Improves **teamwork and communication** 
- Ensures everyone knows their role 
- **Avoids duplicated work** and misunderstandings 
 
## When to use RACI 
- Project management projects 
- Marketing projects 
- School projects 
 
## Example (web development project) 
 
| **task** | **web developer** | **project manager** | **design team** | **company leadership** | 
|------|---------------|------------------|--------------|---------------------| 
| *coding the website* | r | a | c | i | 
| *design implementation* | c | a | r | i | 
| *project oversight* | i | r | c | a | 
| *progress reporting* | c | r | i | a | 
 
## Fun facts
- Created around the 1950s
- Some companies use it for everyday office tasks
 
# Schedule

## Definition of schedule
- Actionable timeline mapping **tasks**, **milestones**, **deliverables**
- Defines sequence of work, start and finish dates, resource allocation
- Created during planning and specification stage
 
## The 6 processes for building a schedule
- Plan schedule management
- Define project activities
- Sequence activities
- Estimate activity durations
- Develop schedule
- Control the schedule

## Ways of presenting a schedule

### [[03 Erazmus/03.07 Project management/03.07.B Tools/GANTT\|Gantt chart]]
- Bar chart showing tasks over time

### Pert diagram
- Network diagram showing dependencies and task durations

## Types of project schedules
- **Master project schedule**
	- Major phases, key deliverables, major milestones
- **Milestone schedule**
	- Only key milestones, no detailed tasks
- **Detailed project schedule**
	- Full breakdown of tasks and activities
	- Often based on WBS

## Difference between schedule and milestone
- **Schedule**
	- Full sequence of events with dates
- **Milestone**
	- Key dates marking progress

## Benefits of using a schedule
- Clear project timeline
- Improved planning and organization
- Better progress tracking
- Efficient resource management
- Enhanced communication

# [[03 Erazmus/03.07 Project management/03.07.B Tools/Communication plan\|Communication plan]]

## Importance of communication
- Team members know what to do
- Aligns goals and expectations
- Keeps stakeholders informed
- Coordinates resources
- Identifies risks
- Builds team cohesion and accountability

## Core components of a communication plan

### Sender
- Initiates communication
- Often the project manager

### Recipients
- Individuals or teams receiving communication

### Description
- Content of the communication
- Includes necessary information and details

### Method
- Communication channel such as email, meeting, or platform

### Frequency
- Daily, weekly, monthly, or as needed

## Communication matrix example

| sender | recipient | description | method | frequency |
|--------|-----------|-------------|---------|------------|
| project manager | core project team | weekly status report | email | fridays at 9 am |
| project manager | core project team | weekly meeting | meeting | wednesday at 11 am |
| project manager | core project team | meeting minutes | sharepoint | as needed |
| marketing lead | customers | product launch notice | email | 2/17/2023 |

## Project phases and communication

### Planning phase
- Outline tasks and expectations
- Delegate communication roles
- Specify frequency
- Choose storage for communication

### Execution and control phase
- Tailor communication to project type
- Monitor situation
- Keep in touch with stakeholders

### Closing phase
- Determine success criteria
- Document lessons learned

# [[03 Erazmus/03.07 Project management/03.07.B Tools/Risk analysis\|Risk analysis]]

## What is risk analysis
- Risk is a problem that can happen
- Analysis means studying it and making a plan
- **Prevents surprises**, **saves money**, **supports timely completion**

### Three steps
- **Find**: list all problems
- **Ask**: probability and impact
- **Fix**: prioritize big problems

## Determining risk level

### Probability
- Low means no
- Medium means maybe
- High means yes

### Impact
- Low means small
- Medium means some
- High means big

### Risk colors
- Red means high priority
- Yellow means monitor
- Green means low concern

## Example (website project)
- **Server breaks**: medium probability, high impact, red
- **Content is late**: high probability, medium impact, red
- **Too expensive**: low probability, high impact, yellow
- **Small bugs**: high probability, low impact, green

## Four ways to handle risks
- **Avoid**
	- Change plan so risk cannot happen
- **Reduce**
	- Make the problem smaller
- **Transfer**
	- Give responsibility to someone else
- **Accept**
	- Do nothing if risk is small

## Pros and cons

### Pros
- Easy to understand
- Prevents surprises
- Saves money
- Improves teamwork

### Cons
- Different opinions
- Takes time
- Cannot identify all risks
- Not 100 percent safe
