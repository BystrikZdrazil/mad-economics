Portfolio management is a **dynamic decision process** where a business’s list of active projects is continuously updated, evaluated, selected, prioritized, accelerated, killed, or resourced

> [!info]
> “Portfolio management is a **dynamic decision process**, whereby a business’s **list** of active new product (and R&D) projects is **constantly up-dated and revised**”

Portfolio management focuses on **doing the right work**, while project and program management focus on **doing work right**.

Portfolio management expresses the **business strategy in action**, because strategy becomes real when resources are allocated.

Portfolio management deals with **uncertain information**, **multiple goals**, **strategic considerations**, **interdependencies**, and **multiple decision-makers**.

# Purpose of Portfolio Management
- Ensures the business selects **the right projects**, not just executes projects well
- Aligns projects with **[[03 Erazmus/03.07 Project management/03.07.A Assignments/Project strategy Nordisk\|strategy]]** and long-term direction
- Maximizes **value**, **return on investment**, and **R&D productivity**
- Ensures **balance** between [[03 Erazmus/03.07 Project management/Types of projects\|short-term and long-term, high-risk and low-risk]] projects
- **Prevents resource overload** by limiting the number of active projects
- Improves **objectivity** in project selection and reduces politics, emotions, and “pet projects”
- Enhances **communication** vertically (*management visibility*) and horizontally (*cross-functional alignment*)

# Importance of Portfolio Management
- **Maintains the competitive position** of the business
- **Ensures** scarce **resources** are **allocated efficiently**
- Links project selection directly to business strategy
- Increases focus by **preventing too many simultaneous projects**
- Ensures the right **balance of project types**
- **Improves communication** across the organization
- Increases **objectivity** and reduces bias in decision-making
- **Maximizes financial return** and R&D productivity

# Consequences of Poor Portfolio Management
- Projects **not aligned with strategy**
- Too many **mediocre** or **low-value projects**
- **Reluctance to kill** weak projects, leading to lack of focus
- Resources **spread too thinly**
- **Decisions based on politics**, **emotions**, or **preferences** instead of facts
- **Long time-to-market** and poor execution quality
- High-value projects starved of resources

# Decision-Making Process
- **Assessment** of all project **commitments**
- Creation of a **detailed inventory of all current**, **proposed**, and **pending projects**
- **Evaluation of projects** using agreed selection criteria
- Prioritization and **scoring of projects**
- **Determination of the optimal project mix** aligned with strategy
- Ongoing **monitoring of project value** and performance
- Making **Go/Kill decisions** at gates or review points

# Typical Evaluation Criteria
- Return on Investment (ROI)
- Expenses and cost structure
- Resource needs
- Risk level
- Time to completion
- Strategic fit
- Expected impact
- Probability of technical and commercial success

# Methods and Tools in Portfolio Management
- **Financial methods**
	- Most widely used method in industry “*77% of businesses use a financial approach*”
	- Includes NPV, ROI, RONA, payback period
	- **Expected Commercial Value** (ECV) is a common model $$ECV = ((NPV × Pcs – C) × Pts)– D$$where:
		- **Pts** = probability of technical success
		- **Pcs** = probability of commercial success
		- **C** = commercialization cost
		- **D** = development cost
    - Projects ranked by ECV or ECV/resource index
- **Strategic methods**
	- Second most popular method “*65% of businesses use a strategic approach*” 
	- Allocates resources into strategic buckets (*e.g., breakthrough, platform, maintenance*)
	- Ensures **alignment with long-term strategy**
	- Prevents financial metrics from dominating decisions
- **Bubble diagrams/portfolio maps**
	- Projects plotted on a 2D map (*e.g., value vs. risk*)
	- Categories often include 
		- **Pearls** -> Low risk // high reward
		- **Oysters** -> High risk // high reward
		- **Bread-and-butter** -> low risk // low reward
		- **White elephants** -> low probability // low value
	- Useful for visualizing balance and distribution of project types
- **Scoring models**
	- Projects rated on multiple criteria (*e.g., strategic fit, technical feasibility, market attractiveness*)
	- Scores summed or weighted to produce a total project score
	- **Improves objectivity** and reduces political influence
- **Checklists**
	- Simple yes/no or criteria-based lists
	- Least sophisticated method
	- Used by ~20% of businesses 

# Success Factors in Portfolio Management
- Use **formal portfolio management systems**
- Combine multiple methods (*financial + strategic + scoring*)
- **Avoid over-reliance on financial** metrics alone
- Build decisions on **strategy**, not just numbers
- Involve and engage **top management**
- Conduct **regular portfolio reviews**
- Maintain **clear Go/Kill criteria**
- Ensure **transparency and communication** across functions

# What Causes Portfolio Management to Fail
- **Lack** of strategic **alignment**
- Weak or **inconsistent Go/Kill decisions**
- Too **many low-value** projects
- **Reluctance to kill** failing projects
- Resource overload
- **No** formal **selection method**
- Decisions influenced by politics or personal preferences

# Project Tools
- **Project charter**
	- Also known as project mandate, **business plan**, or PID
	- Purpose is to support Go/No-Go decisions
	- Prepared by project owner, project team, or management
	- Acts as a contract describing the project
	- **Not updated after approval**
- **Business case**
	- Tool for **comparing project options**
	- Supports strategic prioritization and internal budget approval
	- Used when resources or expenditures need justification
	- **Evaluates** problems, opportunities, benefits, risks, costs, timescale, and capability to deliver
	- Compares **current situation** vs **future vision**
	- Key sections:
		- Executive summary
		- Finance section
		- Project definition
		- Project organization

# Why Portfolio Management Is Difficult
- Requires **balancing many competing priorities**
- Involves **uncertainty and changing information**
- Requires skilled and experienced project managers _“Portfolio management requires an experienced and skilled project manager”_

# Real-World Example (Lecture)
- Real estate maintenance planning over 10 years (*Odense example*)
- Demonstrates how many small tasks become projects in a portfolio

# Takeaways
- Portfolio management is essential for selecting the right projects and aligning them with strategy
- Poor portfolio management leads to wasted resources, weak projects, and strategic drift
- Successful portfolio management requires formal methods, strategic alignment, and strong Go/Kill discipline
- Financial metrics alone are insufficient; strategic and scoring models must complement them
- Communication, transparency, and leadership involvement are critical
