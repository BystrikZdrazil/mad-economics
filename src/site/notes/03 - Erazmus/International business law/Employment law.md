# Purpose & Background
- EU wanted to harmonise minimum standards for informing employees about their employment terms
- Driven by:
    - Growth of new forms of work
    - Differences in national laws
    - Need for transparency in the labour market
    - Social Charter 1989 (employment terms must be written)
- Directive 91/533/EEC = minimum directive (member states may give more protection)

# Scope of the Directive → Art. 1
- Applies to:
    - Every paid employee under Member State law
- Member states may exclude:
    - Employment < 1 month
    - Employment < 8 hours/week
    - Casual/specific employment (if objectively justified)
- Denmark excludes → sailors, <1 month, <8 hours/week, employees already covered by collective agreements

# Employer’s Duty to Inform → Art. 2 / Danish Act §2
- Employers must provide essential terms of employment
- Minimum information:
    - Names/identities of parties
    - Place of work (or statement of variable workplaces)
    - Job title / nature / description
    - Start date
    - Expected duration (if temporary)
    - Paid leave entitlement or method of calculation
    - Notice periods or method of calculation
    - Pay: basic amount, components, frequency
    - Normal working hours
    - Applicable collective agreements
- Extra Danish requirement:
    - Must also disclose any other essential terms, e.g.:
        - Criminal record requirement
        - Security clearance
        - Overtime obligations
- Some items may be given by reference to laws/collective agreements

# Providing Information → Art. 3 / Danish Act §2
- EU → within **2 months**
- Denmark → within **1 month** (more protective)
    - Accepted formats:
    - Written contract
    - Letter of engagement
    - Other written documents (as long as key items are in one document)
- If nothing is provided → employer must issue a written declaration
- If employment ends before deadline → information still required

# Expatriate Employees → Art. 4 / Danish Act §3
- Before departure, employer must provide extra information:
    - Duration abroad
    - Currency of payment
    - Benefits (cash/kind)
    - Repatriation conditions
    - Confirmation of social security certificates (A1)
- Exception: postings ≤ 1 month

# Changes to Terms → Art. 5 / Danish Act §4
- Must be communicated in writing within 1 month
- Not required if change comes from laws/collective agreements already referenced

# Enforcement → Art. 8 / Danish Act §5–6
- EU → member states must ensure employees can enforce rights in court
- Denmark:
    - Employment Committee decides compliance.
    - Employee may claim compensation

## Compensation levels (Danish Act §6):

|**Category**|**Compensation**|
|---|---|
|Excusable, no material effect|0–1,000 DKK|
|Minor non‑conformity|~2,500 DKK (up to 5,000)|
|Risk of dispute|7,500–10,000 DKK|
|Aggravating circumstances|Up to 20 weeks’ pay (rarely > 25,000 DKK)|
|Repeated violations|Case‑specific|

# Retention Tools (Danish law)
- Employers try to retain key employees using:
    - Extended notice periods
        - Allowed under Salaried Employees Act §2(6)
        - Rarely useful
    - Retention bonuses
        - Paid if employee stays until a specific date
        - Supreme Court - not performance‑related → no proportional entitlement if employee resigns early

# Restrictive Clauses → Employment Clauses Act 2016
- Applies to all employees except top executives 
- **Non‑Solicitation Clauses**
    - Restrict contact with former employer’s customers
    - Valid only if:
        - Customer contact within last 12 months
        - Employee employed ≥ 6 months
        - Written information provided
        - Compensation paid
    - Max. duration → 12 months
    - Employee receives list of affected customers
- **Non‑Competition Clauses**
    - Restrict working for competitors.
    - Valid only if:
        - Employee holds a **specially trusted position**
        - Written justification provided
        - Employee employed ≥ 6 months
        - Compensation paid
    - Max. duration → 12 months
- **Combined Clauses**
    - Non‑competition + non‑solicitation
    - Rules:
        - Max. duration: 6 months
        - Compensation → 60% of salary
        - First 2 months paid as lump sum
        - Remaining months reducible to 24% if employee finds other work
- **Compensation Rules** (*all clauses*)
    - Based on total salary package (incl. car, phone, etc.)
    - First 2 months always lump sum
    - 40% compensation → reducible to minimum 16%
    - 60% compensation → reducible to minimum 24%
- **Cancellation & Lapse**
    - Employer may cancel clause with 1 month’s notice
    - If employer dismisses employee **without cause**, non‑competition part becomes invalid
    - Non‑solicitation part remains valid
    - Clause invalid if employer materially breaches obligations
- **Job Clauses**
    - Agreements between employers not to hire each other’s employees.
    - Generally prohibited since 2016
    - Exception → during business transfer negotiations (max 6 months)
- **Royalty Clauses**
    - Employee may take over customers but pays % of turnover to former employer.
    - Not regulated
    - Rare
- **Education Clauses**
    - Employer pays for expensive training → employee must stay for a period or repay costs.
    - Not regulated
    - Common for pilots, MBAs, IT certifications

# Posting of Workers (EU Posting Directive + Danish rules)

## 10.1 Definition
Posted worker = employee temporarily working in another Member State.

## 10.2 Applicable Law
- Default: law of the country where employee **habitually works**
- Posting does **not** change applicable law
- Parties may choose host‑country law, but cannot deprive employee of mandatory protections

## 10.3 Minimum Host‑Country Protections (Art. 3)
Posting employer must ensure host‑country standards for:
- Maximum work periods / minimum rest
- Minimum paid holidays
- Minimum pay (incl. overtime)
- Conditions for hiring out workers
- Health & safety
- Protection of pregnant women, children, adolescents
- Non‑discrimination

## 10.4 Enforcement Directive (2014) — Chain Liability
Host company can be held responsible if posting company underpays workers.

## 10.5 Social Security (A1 Certificate)
Posted worker remains under home‑country social security if:
- Posting ≤ 24 months
- Employment contract exists in home country
- Worker previously worked for employer
- Employer retains instruction power
- Worker does not replace another posted worker

If A1 not possible → authorities may agree on exception.

## 10.6 Posting Addendum (Art. 4 / Danish §3)
Before departure, employee must receive:
- Duration abroad
- Currency of salary
- Benefits (cash/kind)
- Repatriation conditions
- Confirmation of social security certificates
