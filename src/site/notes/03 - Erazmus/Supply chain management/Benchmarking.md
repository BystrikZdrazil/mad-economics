## Quantitative objectives
- To fully integrate SCM in the BS framework, it’s important to incorporate quantitative measures

> [!example]
>- Supply chain efficiency
>	- Total value added time = **10**
>	- Total time in the supply chain = **50**
>	- Supply chain cycle efficiency = **10/50=0,2**
- Number of choices offered relative to response time
	- A measure showing the efficiency of the supply chains ability to offer variety without lengthening the time it takes to create this variety
	- Measurement example
		- Choices offered = **20**
		- Response time = **4 days**
		- Number of choices relative to response time = **20/4=5**

>[!abstract]+ Key terms and definitions
>**Balanced Scorecard (BSC)**
>A strategic performance management framework that combines financial and nonfinancial measures across four perspectives: Financial, Customer, Internal Process, and Learning & Growth.
>
>**Strategy Map**
>A visual representation showing cause‑and‑effect relationships among strategic objectives across the four BSC perspectives.
>
>**Lagging indicators**
>Measures that reflect outcomes of past actions (e.g., financial results).
>
>**Leading indicators**
>Measures that predict future performance (e.g., employee training, process improvements).
>
>**Value Proposition**
>The unique mix of benefits a company offers customers (e.g., low cost, product leadership, customer solutions).
>
>**Strategic Objective**
>A short action phrase describing what the organization aims to achieve (e.g., “Improve process quality”).
>
>**Measure (Metric)**
>A quantitative indicator used to assess performance on a strategic objective.
>
>**Target**
>The desired level of performance for a measure.
>
>**Human Capital**
>Employee skills, knowledge, and capabilities.
>
>**Information Capital**
>Systems, databases, and technology supporting operations and strategy.
>
>**Organizational Capital**
>Culture, leadership, alignment, and teamwork that support strategy execution.

# Balanced Scorecard and Strategy Map

## Purpose and rationale
- Companies must create long‑term value, not only short‑term financial results.
- Financial measures alone are insufficient because they are lagging indicators and ignore intangible assets.
- Modern value creation depends on customer relationships, process quality, innovation, employee skills, and information systems.
- Measurement must support strategy: “If you don’t measure it, you can’t manage it.”

## Why both financial and nonfinancial measures are needed
- Financial metrics show the results of past decisions.
- Nonfinancial metrics capture drivers of future performance (customer satisfaction, process quality, innovation, employee capabilities).
- Spending on intangible assets often reduces short‑term profit but increases long‑term value; financial statements do not capture this.

# The Balanced Scorecard Framework

## The four perspectives
**Financial perspective**
- Measures whether strategy improves bottom‑line results.
- Focus areas: productivity improvements and revenue growth.
- Typical measures: ROE, ROI/ROA, profit margins, turnover ratios, revenue growth, share price, liquidity and solvency ratios.

**Customer perspective**
- Identifies target customer segments and how the company creates value for them.
- Typical outcomes: customer satisfaction, loyalty, acquisition, market share, customer profitability.
- Value propositions:
    - Low total cost (operational excellence)
    - Product leadership
    - Customer solutions / intimacy

**Internal Process perspective**
- Identifies critical processes needed to deliver the customer value proposition and achieve financial goals.
- Four process groups:
    1. Operations processes (day‑to‑day production and delivery)
    2. Customer processes (marketing, sales, relationship building)
    3. Innovation processes (new products, improved processes)
    4. Regulatory and social processes (compliance, responsibility)

**Learning and Growth perspective**
- Provides the foundation for improving processes and customer relationships.
- Three components:
    - Human capital (skills, training, motivation)
    - Information capital (systems, data, IT support)
    - Organizational capital (culture, alignment, leadership, knowledge sharing)

# Cause‑and‑effect logic in the BSC
- Learning & Growth improvements → better Internal Processes
- Better Internal Processes → improved Customer outcomes
- Improved Customer outcomes → stronger Financial performance
- Financial and customer measures represent the “what” of strategy.
- Process and learning/growth represent the “how.”

> [!example]
>- Better employee skills → improved process quality → on‑time delivery → customer retention → higher ROE.
>- Discount Airlines: faster ground turnaround → on‑time arrivals + higher aircraft utilization → low prices + revenue growth → higher profits.

# Strategy and the BSC

## What strategy means
- Describes how a company uses internal capabilities to deliver superior value to targeted customers.
- Clarifies competitive advantage and scope (segments, geography, product lines).
- Guides resource allocation and employee decision‑making.

## Role of the strategy map
- Translates strategy into linked objectives across the four perspectives.
- Makes hypotheses explicit: “If we improve X, it will lead to Y.”
- Helps communicate strategy throughout the organization.

# Objectives, Measures, and Targets

## Strategic objectives
- Short action phrases describing what the organization wants to achieve.
- Should reflect cause‑and‑effect logic.
- Examples from the documents:
    - Retain customers by delivering on time
    - Improve process quality
    - Develop employees’ improvement skills

## Measures
- Specify how each objective will be evaluated.
- Reduce ambiguity (e.g., defining “on time” precisely).
- Provide focus and enable target setting.

## Targets
- Define desired performance levels.
- Should represent excellent performance and position the company among industry leaders.
- Used for communication, motivation, feedback, and evaluation.

# Real‑world application
- Around 60% of organizations in U.S. surveys use the BSC; somewhat lower in Denmark but still widely applied.
- Some companies adopt the full model; others use modified or simplified versions.
- BSC is especially useful for aligning large, geographically dispersed organizations (e.g., Pioneer Petroleum case).

>[!example]+
>**Small manufacturing company**
>- Strategy: low cost, high quality, on‑time delivery.
>- Linked objectives:
>	- Improve process cycle time and quality → on‑time delivery + competitive prices → customer retention → increased ROE.
>
>**Discount Airlines**
>- Strategy: low prices + reliable on‑time performance.
>- Key process: reduce ground turnaround time.
>- Learning & Growth: train and motivate ground crew.
>- Customer: attract and retain customers through low prices and punctuality.
>- Financial: increase profits, ROI, revenue per mile, and asset utilization.

# Dos and don'ts
## 1. Lack of Management Support
- The Balanced Scorecard (BSC) is **resource‑intensive** and touches the deepest parts of the organization.
- Without **visible, active executive sponsorship**, the BSC becomes a side project rather than a strategic driver.
- Leadership must champion the process, allocate resources, and model commitment.

## 2. Treating BSC as a One‑Time Project
- Many organizations assume BSC is something you “implement and finish.”
- In reality, it is a **continuous strategic management system** requiring ongoing review, refinement, and alignment.
- Failure occurs when organizations stop after initial setup.

## 3. Poor Communication
- Employees often don’t understand _why_ the BSC is being implemented or _how_ it affects them.
- Lack of communication leads to resistance, confusion, and low engagement.
- Successful BSC requires **transparent, repeated communication** across all levels.

## 4. Too Many Measures
- Overloading the Scorecard with dozens of KPIs dilutes focus.
- The BSC should highlight **critical strategic measures**, not everything that can be measured.
- Too many KPIs → analysis paralysis and loss of strategic clarity.

## 5. No Clear Strategy Map
- Some organizations jump straight into KPIs without defining the **cause‑and‑effect logic** of their strategy.
- A Strategy Map is essential to show how objectives connect across perspectives.
- Without it, KPIs become disconnected metrics rather than a coherent system.

## 6. Lack of Alignment Across Departments
- Departments may interpret strategy differently or create their own KPIs that don’t support the enterprise strategy.
- Cascading must be **structured and consistent**, ensuring alignment from top to bottom.
- Misalignment leads to siloed efforts and conflicting priorities.

## 7. Infrequent Performance Reviews
- Quarterly or annual reviews are too slow to drive strategic execution.
- BSC requires **regular (monthly) review cycles**, with weekly operational check‑ins for key processes.
- Infrequent reviews cause loss of momentum and delayed corrective action.

## 8. Unclear Metrics & Data Problems
- KPIs must be clearly defined, measurable, and supported by reliable data.
- Ambiguous metrics or inconsistent data collection undermine trust in the system.
- Automating data collection and defining ownership improves reliability.

## 9. Focusing Only on Measurement (Not Strategy Execution)
- Many organizations mistakenly use the BSC as a **reporting tool** rather than a **strategy execution framework**.
- The BSC is not about tracking numbers—it is about driving strategic change.
- Without linking KPIs to decisions, initiatives, and resource allocation, the BSC fails.

## 10. Ignoring Change Management
- Implementing BSC changes how people plan, measure, and make decisions.
- Without proper change management—training, communication, stakeholder engagement—resistance grows.
- Successful BSC requires cultural adoption, not just technical setup.

# The BS vs SCM
- **Customer Perspective**
	- **Goals**: customer view of product or service
	- **Measures**: number of customer contact points
- **Internal business perspective**
	- **Goals**: flexible response
	- **Measures**: Number of choices or average response time
