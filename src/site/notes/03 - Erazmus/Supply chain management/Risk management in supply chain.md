# Risk management basics
**Risk management** is an intuitive and natural process that can be applied to any situation, from personal decisions to complex organizational projects. Rather than being a strictly bureaucratic or statistical exercise, it is built around asking and answering **six basic questions**:
1. **What are we trying to achieve?** This first step involves **objective setting**, where you define the scope, context, and environment of your goal.
2. **What might affect us?** This is **risk identification**, focusing on future uncertainties that could either hinder your progress or provide helpful opportunitie.
3. **Which are the most important?** During **risk assessment**, you prioritize risks based on two dimensions: how likely they are to happen and what effect (positive or negative) they would have on your goals.
4. **What should we do about it?** This involves **designing responses**. For "bad risks" (threats), you aim to avoid or minimize them; for "good risks" (opportunities), you look for ways to exploit or maximize them.
5. **Did it work?** Because planned actions don't always achieve the intended result, you must monitor your progress and **modify your approach** if necessary.
6. **What has changed?** Projects and businesses are dynamic, so it is vital to continuously spot **new risks** that have arisen since the last time you took action.

At its root, risk management is about using these questions to ensure you are not just a "victim of uncertainty". While large-scale projects may use complex tools and modeling to answer them, the **fundamental process remains the same** regardless of the project's size.

# Supply chain vulnerability
There are a few key questions to identify vulnerabilities in the supply chain
- *What has disrupted the business before?*
- *What known weaknesses do we have?*
- *What “near misses” have we experienced?*

Applying even more proactive approach:
- *What is the impact of missing (key) materials?*
- *What is the impact of losing a distribution channel?*
- *What s the impact of losing a key supplier/customer?*

# Supply chain risk management
It refers to the strategies that companies/supply chains use to identify and manage risk in the supply chain.

> [!example]
> **Increased visibility** between suppliers and customers through information sharing
> 
> **Increased flexibility** in the production process
> 
> **Postponement**
> 
> **Multiple sourcing** (*suppliers*) and flexible contracts
> 
> **Increased buffers**, for example through safety stock
> 
> **Joint planning** and **coordination** with other **supply chain partners**

# Different types of risks
- Systemic
- Market
- Operational
- Credit and liquidity

| **Type of risk**       | **Impact**                              | **Sources**                                      | **Example**                                                                                                                                                          |
| ---------------------- | --------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------ | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| *Systemic*             | On the general economy                  | Political uncertainties                          | Situations of political instability, changes in government policy, wars, terrorism, coups d’état, piracy                                                             |
|                        |                                         | Macroeconomic uncertainties                      | Fluctuations in levels of economic activity, or relative prices                                                                                                      |
|                        |                                         | Social uncertainties                             | Changes in peoples’ values, attitudes or beliefs                                                                                                                     |
|                        |                                         | Environmental uncertainties                      | Biothreats, floods, droughts, earthquakes, hurricanes                                                                                                                |
| *Market*               | On a specific sector of the economy     | Market uncertainties                             | Fluctuations in price levels of inputs and products, technological changes, changes in consumer preferences, availability of alternative products                    |
|                        |                                         | Regulatory or institutional uncertainties        | Quality standards and regulations, changes in the specific regulations of the sector                                                                                 |
| *Operational*          | On a specific supply chain              | Supply uncertainties                             | Delays in deliveries, failures in input quantity or quality                                                                                                          |
|                        |                                         | Production uncertainties                         | Mechanical, technical or process failures, forecasting errors, infrastructure failures, failures in product quantity or quality                                      |
|                        |                                         | Administrative uncertainties                     | Failures or delays in administrative procedures, such as import and export, compliance with quality standards                                                        |
| *Credit and liquidity* | On a specific supply chain or its nodes | Uncertainty about the sector or the firm segment | Sectors with greater information asymmetry, such as agriculture and new technologies; firm segments such as SMEs, with greater information asymmetry and informality |
|                        |                                         | Uncertainty about the payment cycle              | Non-compliance or extensions in payment cycles that can cause delays in the firm’s short-term commitments                                                            |
|                        |                                         | Uncertainty about the firm’s financial health    | Incomplete or out-of-date financial and accounting records, with low information quality                                                                             |
