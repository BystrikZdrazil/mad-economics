# Which commodities are especially important for Microsoft?
Microsoft is not a manufacturing‑heavy company, so it does **not** rely on classic commodities like oil, wheat, or metals in the same way industrial firms do.

However, the annual report shows that Microsoft is exposed to **indirect commodity‑linked inputs**, especially through its hardware and cloud infrastructure.

## Key commodity‑linked inputs for Microsoft
- **Energy (electricity)** — critical for data centers and cloud operations (Microsoft highlights energy cost volatility as a risk in its filings)
- **Semiconductors / chips** — not a commodity in the traditional sense, but treated as a critical input with supply‑chain volatility
- **Raw materials for hardware** (Surface, Xbox, accessories):
    - Rare earth elements
    - Metals (aluminum, copper, gold)
    - Plastics and petrochemical derivatives
- **Cloud infrastructure materials** (servers, networking equipment) — again tied to metals and energy

Microsoft does **not** list specific commodities by name, but the risk disclosures clearly point to **energy and hardware‑related materials** as the main exposures.

# How have the prices of those commodities developed?
Since Microsoft’s main exposures are **energy and hardware‑related materials**, here is the general trend context:

## Energy (electricity, natural gas)
- Global energy prices have been volatile since 2021
- Electricity prices for data centers have risen in many regions
- Microsoft explicitly notes cost pressure from energy markets in its filings

## Semiconductor‑related materials
- Chip shortages from 2020–2023 caused elevated prices
- Supply has improved, but prices remain above pre‑pandemic levels

## Metals (copper, aluminum, rare earths)
- Copper and aluminum have shown multi‑year upward trends due to electrification and AI‑driven demand
- Rare earth elements remain volatile due to geopolitical concentration

These trends matter because they directly affect **data center expansion costs** and **hardware margins**.

# Does Microsoft mention how they manage commodity price risk?
Yes — but indirectly.

Microsoft does **not** hedge commodities directly (like an airline hedges fuel).

Instead, the annual report describes **broader cost‑management and supply‑chain risk strategies**, including:

- Long‑term supplier agreements
- Diversified sourcing
- Inventory and supply‑chain management
- Energy procurement strategies
- Multi‑year contracts for cloud infrastructure inputs
- Operational efficiency programs

The 10‑K discusses cost risks related to **energy**, **components**, and **supply‑chain constraints**, but does **not** describe commodity derivatives or futures hedging.

This indicates Microsoft uses **operational hedging**, not financial hedging.

# Advantages and disadvantages of different hedging methods

|Method|Advantages|Disadvantages|
|---|---|---|
|**Inventory hedging**|protects against short‑term price spikes; ensures supply|ties up liquidity; storage costs; risk of obsolescence|
|**Vertical integration**|strong supply security; reduces dependency on suppliers|extremely capital‑intensive; not feasible for Microsoft|
|**Forward contracts**|locks in prices; eliminates uncertainty|no upside potential; counterparty risk (unless exchange‑traded)|
|**Futures contracts**|liquid; transparent pricing; eliminates credit risk|requires margin; daily mark‑to‑market affects liquidity|
|**Options**|protection with upside potential; flexible|premium cost; more complex|
|**Operational hedging (Microsoft’s approach)**|avoids financial risk; flexible; scalable|does not fully eliminate price volatility; dependent on supplier relationships|

# Which hedging methods would you recommend for Microsoft?
Given Microsoft’s business model, the best hedging strategy is **operational**, not financial.
1. Long‑term supplier contracts**
	- Stabilizes prices for chips, servers, and cloud infrastructure
	- Already used by Microsoft and should be expanded
2. Energy procurement hedging (operational + financial)**
	- Long‑term power purchase agreements (PPAs)
	- Renewable energy contracts
	- Partial financial hedging for electricity where feasible
3. Supply‑chain diversification**
	- Reduces geopolitical and commodity‑specific risk
	- Especially important for rare earths and semiconductors
4. Avoid financial commodity futures**
	- Microsoft does not consume commodities in large, standardized volumes
	- Futures would not match their actual exposures (basis risk)
5. Use options selectively
	- For large, one‑off infrastructure investments where price spikes could materially affect budgets
